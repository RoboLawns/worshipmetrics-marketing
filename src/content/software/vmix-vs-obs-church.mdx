Both vMix and OBS are excellent streaming software, and the choice between them comes down to your church's specific needs, budget, and technical capacity. Neither is universally "better"—they're different tools for different situations. This guide compares them directly so you can make an informed decision.

## Price and Licensing

OBS is free. vMix costs $260/year (Pro tier, the standard for churches). That's the simplest comparison, but cost goes deeper. OBS is free but often requires expensive hardware to run well—you might need to upgrade your PC to an i9 to handle what a vMix setup handles on an i7. vMix costs $260/year but runs on less expensive hardware, potentially offsetting the subscription cost.

For a small church streaming for the first time, OBS's free price removes barriers. You can try streaming without financial commitment. If your volunteer team already has a decent PC (i7, 16GB RAM), OBS is the economic choice.

For a church that's committed to streaming long-term and needs professional features, vMix's subscription is an affordable ongoing cost. Divided across the year, $260/year is about $5/week—less than your coffee budget.

## Features and Capabilities

OBS covers the basics: multiple camera inputs, scene management, NDI support (via plugin), audio mixing, streaming to YouTube and Facebook, recording, and third-party graphics integration. It's a solid feature set for standard church productions.

vMix includes everything OBS has, plus replay, built-in title system (GT), ATEM hardware control, NDI tally lights, better graphics support, and more simultaneous inputs. These extras transform advanced productions but add complexity. If you're doing basic streaming (one or two cameras, simple graphics), vMix's advanced features sit unused.

For features, it comes down to: does your church need replay? Do you want native title design (not browser sources)? Do you want tally lights for camera operators? If yes to any of these, vMix is significantly better. If no, OBS is sufficient.

## Hardware Requirements

OBS runs on modest hardware: an i5 with 8GB RAM handles 720p streaming. vMix genuinely needs an i7 with 16GB RAM for the same production. If your church has a spare computer from 2015, OBS might run; vMix will struggle.

Conversely, if your church is buying a new production PC, the difference in cost between an i7 and i5 system is only $200–300. For that marginal cost, you get vMix's capabilities. The decision becomes less about "which software requires less hardware" and more about "what PC am I building?"

GPU matters more in vMix than OBS. OBS can use integrated graphics. vMix appreciates a dedicated GPU. If your current PC lacks discrete graphics, adding one ($250–400) alongside vMix makes sense.

## Learning Curve

OBS is simpler to learn. A motivated volunteer can be productive in OBS within 1–2 hours. vMix takes longer—most people need 4–6 hours of training plus 10–20 hours of practice to become comfortable.

However, this difference matters less than it appears. By hour 10 of practice in either software, most volunteers are competent. By hour 20, they're proficient. OBS gets people to competence faster, but vMix gets them to mastery equally fast—just starts higher.

The real difference is ongoing. OBS's simplicity means new volunteers pick it up quickly every time you onboard someone. vMix's complexity means each new volunteer requires dedicated training, which takes more of your technical director's time.

For churches with stable volunteer teams (same director week after week), this doesn't matter. For churches with rotating volunteers, OBS's lower learning curve is valuable.

## Community and Resources

OBS has an enormous community. Tutorials, forum posts, and plugins are abundant. If you get stuck, odds are someone online has answered your question.

vMix has a smaller but dedicated community. Official documentation is excellent, and forums are responsive. If you search for a specific technique and don't find it, vMix's support staff usually responds within 24 hours.

For most common problems (audio setup, streaming configuration, basic troubleshooting), both have resources. For advanced techniques, vMix's documentation is cleaner because vMix is more focused software. OBS's breadth means more general information but sometimes less specific detail.

## Reliability During Live Service

OBS is very reliable. Thousands of churches stream with OBS every week without issues. If you configure it correctly (proper bitrate, encoding settings, audio levels), it's rock-solid.

vMix is equally reliable, with additional confidence features. vMix's hardware encoding means you're less likely to hit CPU limits. vMix's dashboard gives better visibility into what's happening (frame rate per platform, detailed stats). vMix's design assumes mission-critical use, so edge cases are handled more gracefully.

In practice, both are reliable enough for church use. If reliability is your primary concern, both are equally good choices. The difference is that vMix gives you more visibility and margin, which some find reassuring.

## When to Choose OBS

Choose OBS if you're new to streaming and want to test viability without commitment. If you have a modest budget and a capable volunteer, OBS is sufficient for years.

Choose OBS if your church values simplicity and your productions are straightforward (1–2 cameras, simple graphics). OBS doesn't force complexity on you.

Choose OBS if you have rotating volunteer directors. The low learning curve means onboarding is fast.

Choose OBS if your current PC is older and you can't afford hardware upgrades. OBS runs on less powerful systems.

## When to Choose vMix

Choose vMix if you're committed to growing your production. vMix's feature set scales with your ambition.

Choose vMix if you want instant replay. This single feature is transformative for engaging productions.

Choose vMix if your church has more than three cameras. vMix handles input management more elegantly than OBS.

Choose vMix if your church is building a new production PC anyway. The hardware cost difference is minimal, and vMix's capabilities add value.

Choose vMix if you want built-in title design without third-party tools. vMix's GT system is simpler than OBS's browser source approach.

Choose vMix if your church already has ATEM hardware or NDI cameras with tally lights. vMix's integration is native and polished.

## The Honest Comparison

Both are excellent software. The difference is philosophical: OBS is general-purpose streaming software used by gamers, conferences, and churches. vMix is purpose-built for live production (churches, weddings, events, broadcasts). vMix is more specialized and assumes live production use cases that OBS doesn't always anticipate.

For 80% of churches, OBS is the right answer. It's free, proven, sufficient, and has enormous community support. Master OBS and you have excellent streaming.

For 20% of churches with ambitious productions, vMix becomes the right answer. It does things OBS doesn't, and it does them more elegantly. The subscription cost is justified by features that scale with your program.

There's no universal "best choice." The best choice is the one that fits your church's size, budget, technical skills, and production ambitions. Think honestly about where your church is today and where it wants to be in three years. That answer guides your decision.
