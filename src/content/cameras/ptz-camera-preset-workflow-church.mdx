The foundation of professional-looking PTZ camera operation is a well-planned preset workflow. Presets allow your camera to move to specific positions instantly, providing consistent framing throughout your service. This guide covers developing a repeatable workflow that makes your Sunday broadcasts smooth and polished.

## Understanding the Power of Presets

Presets store the camera's pan, tilt, and zoom position. When you recall a preset, the camera smoothly moves to that exact position. This repeatability is what makes broadcasts look professional.

Without presets, your camera operator would need to manually adjust the camera during each service, potentially creating jerky movements or inconsistent framing. Presets eliminate this variability and create a polished, intentional look.

## Service Structure Analysis

Start by analyzing your actual service structure. How long is your service? What are the distinct segments (opening, worship, announcements, sermon, closing)? Which people or groups are on stage during each segment?

For a typical 60-minute service, you might have: opening song (5 min), pastoral greeting (5 min), worship set (15 min), announcements (5 min), sermon (25 min), closing prayer and song (5 min). Each segment might use different camera shots.

Document your service structure in detail. Include timing if available. This becomes the framework for your preset planning.

## Identifying Required Shots

For each segment, identify what shots are important. For worship, you might want the band in frame. For the sermon, you want the pastor. For announcements, you might want a medium shot showing both the speaker and the stage context.

Discuss shot selection with your worship leader. They can tell you what they want to see in the broadcast and what angles feel natural for your service style.

Most churches benefit from having 8 to 12 presets for a complete Sunday service. Avoid excessive preset changing—too many shot changes are distracting. Instead, hold on one shot for 15 to 30 seconds before switching.

## Positioning the Camera for Presets

Once you've identified what shots you need, physically position your camera for each preset. Start with your wide opening shot—this typically shows your entire stage and some congregation.

Zoom in or out to frame the shot as you want viewers to see it. Adjust pan and tilt so the important elements are properly centered. Take your time positioning each shot correctly.

## Saving Presets Systematically

Save presets in the order you'll use them during your service. Preset 1 is your opening shot, preset 2 is your next shot, etc. This systematic approach prevents confusion about which preset is which.

As you save each preset, give it a descriptive name if your camera software allows it. Names like "Opening Wide," "Pastor Sermon," "Worship Band" are more meaningful than "Preset 1" and "Preset 2."

## Documenting Your Preset Plan

Create a written reference document showing your preset plan. Include:
- Preset number
- Preset name/description
- What the shot shows
- Which segment of the service uses this preset
- Approximate timing

Printed or digital copies of this document help your camera operator know which preset to use and when.

## Creating a Control Sheet for Operators

Develop a control sheet that shows the sequence of presets throughout your service. It might look like:

1. Opening Song → Preset 1 (Wide Stage)
2. Pastoral Greeting → Preset 2 (Pastor Center)
3. Worship Band Song → Preset 3 (Band Tight)
4. etc.

During your service, your operator follows this sheet, recalling presets at appropriate moments. Over time, operators memorize the sequence and the sheet becomes a backup reference.

## Rehearsal and Refinement

Before using presets in a live service, rehearse with your entire team. Have your camera operator call out when they're transitioning between presets. Watch the resulting broadcast to verify that transitions happen at appropriate times.

You'll often discover during rehearsal that a preset needs adjustment. Maybe the zoom is slightly too tight or the framing isn't centered. Make these adjustments and resave the preset.

Rehearse multiple times if possible. Each rehearsal helps your team refine the workflow and gain confidence.

## Operator Training

Train your camera operators on the preset system. Walk them through each preset, showing them what they'll see and where they'll need to be in the sequence.

Have operators practice recalling presets in order, developing muscle memory. Some operators prefer to follow a printed sheet, while others memorize the sequence over time.

The best camera operators understand the service flow well enough to predict when the next preset change will happen, making the transitions feel natural and intentional.

## Managing Preset Changes

As your service evolves, update your presets. If a speaker moves the pulpit slightly, you might need to adjust your pulpit close-up preset. If your stage setup changes seasonally, your presets will need updating.

Before major holiday services or special events, plan for potential preset changes needed by the new stage setup. Don't assume last year's presets will work this year.

## Multiple Service Support

If you have multiple services (traditional and contemporary, for example), different services might need different presets. Save multiple preset sets labeled for each service type.

Many cameras allow you to save multiple preset groups. Organize them clearly so you load the correct preset group each week.

## Handling Unexpected Changes

Despite careful planning, unexpected changes happen. A speaker stands in an unfamiliar location. A musician plays from an unexpected area of the stage. A technical issue occurs.

Train your operators to respond by either using the closest matching preset or taking manual control to frame the unexpected situation. Have operators understand that slight imperfection is acceptable—perfection is less important than keeping the broadcast moving.

## Workflow Optimization Over Time

As your team runs services with the preset system, you'll naturally optimize the workflow. Maybe some presets are never used and could be removed. Maybe you discover you need additional presets for transitions.

Track what's working and what isn't. Make incremental improvements to your preset plan over months, not all at once.

## Backup Manual Control

While presets are ideal, ensure your operators understand how to manually control the camera if presets fail. This backup capability prevents a single failure from derailing your broadcast.

During rehearsal, practice manual camera control so operators are confident they can recover from any preset system issue.

## Documentation and Knowledge Transfer

Document your preset system thoroughly. When volunteers replace experienced operators, good documentation helps them learn quickly.

Include the service structure, preset descriptions, control sheets, and any special notes about specific presets. This documentation becomes invaluable as your team changes over time.

## Continuous Improvement

View your preset system as a living thing that evolves with your ministry. What works well for a traditional service might not work for a contemporary service. Presets that were perfect last year might need adjustment as your team and technology evolve.

Regularly ask yourself: Are presets being used effectively? Do we have too many or too few? Are transitions happening at appropriate times? Use this feedback to continuously improve your system.

## Conclusion

A well-designed preset workflow transforms your PTZ cameras from complicated equipment into reliable tools that make your broadcast look professional. By systematically planning your presets, training your operators, and refining your workflow over time, you'll create broadcasts that look intentional, professional, and polished. Your church's ability to tell its story through video improves dramatically when camera operation is smooth and consistent.
