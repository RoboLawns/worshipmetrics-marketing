The TONGVEO PTZ-2080 is a versatile PTZ camera that gives your church flexibility in how you connect it. Unlike the NDI model that relies entirely on network connectivity, the 2080 offers HDMI for switchers and SDI-like installations, plus USB connectivity for direct integration with computers running OBS or other broadcast software. This guide walks you through the unboxing, initial power-up, and first connections.

## What's in the Box

When your PTZ-2080 arrives, carefully inspect the packaging for any signs of shipping damage. Inside you'll find the camera body with the pan-tilt head already mounted, a power supply with a standard outlet connector, an HDMI cable, a USB cable, and documentation. The HDMI and USB cables included are usually basic versions—if you need longer cables for your installation, plan to purchase higher-quality aftermarket options.

Set the camera on a stable work surface near where you plan to mount it. Before permanently installing it, test all connections and verify that the basic features work. This preliminary testing on a desk is much easier than troubleshooting a camera that's already mounted 15 feet above your congregation.

## Powering On the Camera

Connect the power supply to the back of the camera and plug it into an outlet. You should see indicator lights illuminate on the back panel within a few seconds. The TONGVEO will perform a startup sequence where the pan-tilt mechanism might move slightly as the motors initialize. This is normal behavior and nothing to worry about.

Let the camera fully boot up for about 15 seconds before you try to operate it. The lens might focus or zoom automatically during startup, and you might hear small motor movements as the camera prepares for operation. Once the lights have settled into a steady pattern, the camera is ready to use.

## Connecting HDMI for Video Output

The TONGVEO PTZ-2080 outputs video via HDMI, making it compatible with any professional video switcher, capture card, or monitor. If you're using a Blackmagic ATEM Mini, vMix, or similar switcher that accepts HDMI input, connect the camera to one of your switcher's HDMI inputs using the included HDMI cable.

Plug one end of the HDMI cable into the HDMI port on the back of the camera and the other end into an available HDMI input on your switcher or capture device. Once connected, your switcher should recognize the camera as a video input within a few seconds. You might need to refresh the input list in your switcher's software, but most modern switchers auto-detect HDMI inputs immediately.

If you're using HDMI, keep in mind that the cable length matters. HDMI works reliably up to about 25 feet with standard quality cables, but longer runs might require active HDMI extenders or higher-grade cables. If your camera is mounted far from your switcher, discuss cable routing with your IT and installation team before you start running cables.

## USB Connection for Computer-Based Streaming

If you're using a computer-based streaming solution like OBS Studio or Streamlabs, you can connect the PTZ-2080 directly to your broadcast computer via USB. Plug the USB cable into the USB port on the back of the camera and into a USB port on your computer. Windows and Mac systems should recognize the camera automatically, though you might need to download drivers from the TONGVEO website.

Once the camera is connected and drivers are installed, it will appear as a video input device in your broadcast software. In OBS, go to Sources, add a Video Capture Device, and select the TONGVEO from the dropdown list. The camera will appear in your scene, and you can resize and position it as needed.

USB connection gives you the advantage of not needing a separate HDMI switcher or capture card. Your computer sees the camera as a standard webcam, making it compatible with Zoom, Teams, and other meeting software as well. If you're a smaller church that wants to keep technology simple, USB is an excellent choice.

## Initial Positioning and Pan-Tilt Testing

With the camera powered on and connected to either HDMI or USB, test the pan-tilt movement. Most TONGVEO models include a simple joystick control or arrow buttons on the camera body itself, or they can be controlled through software. Gently move the pan-tilt head to verify that all movements are smooth and responsive.

Move the camera all the way left, then all the way right, to check the full pan range. Do the same for tilt, moving up and down. The camera should move smoothly without jerking or sticking. If you notice any grinding sounds or resistance, power off the camera immediately and inspect for any manufacturing defects or packaging materials caught in the mechanism.

## Zoom and Focus Testing

Test the zoom functionality by zooming all the way in and all the way out. The 20x zoom on the 2080 should provide a smooth range from wide-angle framing of your entire stage to a tight close-up of a speaker's face. The focus should adjust automatically as you zoom.

If the image appears blurry at any zoom level, access the camera's control interface or software settings and look for an autofocus or manual focus adjustment. Most PTZ cameras have excellent autofocus, but in low-light church environments, you might occasionally need to adjust the focus slightly. This is something your camera operator will learn quickly with regular use.

## Connecting to Control Software

If your TONGVEO came with dedicated control software, install it on your broadcast computer or operator station. This software provides an interface for controlling pan, tilt, and zoom, and it often includes preset buttons for saving specific camera positions. Some models also support network control, allowing you to control the camera from anywhere on your church network.

Read the documentation to understand which control options are available for your specific model. Some churches prefer to control the camera through their broadcast software (like vMix, which has excellent PTZ support), while others prefer a dedicated app. Whatever method you choose, test it thoroughly in your rehearsal before relying on it during a Sunday service.

## Cable Management and Rough Placement

Before you permanently mount your camera, plan how the power cable and HDMI or USB cable will route from the camera to your equipment. Use cable trays, conduit, or adhesive cable management clips to keep cables organized and safe. Avoid creating trip hazards or running cables in ways that could be damaged.

If your camera will be mounted on the ceiling, you might need to route cables through the ceiling space or along the perimeter of your sanctuary. Discuss cable routing with whoever is responsible for your building's electrical and infrastructure—you don't want to accidentally run your camera cables through the same conduit as high-voltage power lines.

## Conclusion

Your TONGVEO PTZ-2080 is now powered on, connected, and ready for testing. Whether you're using HDMI to a switcher or USB to a computer, the camera is flexible enough to fit into most church broadcast setups. The next steps involve permanent mounting, preset configuration, and integration with your full production workflow. Refer to other articles in this knowledge base for those advanced topics.
