## Introduction

Presets on the Tenveo VHD30U transform the camera from a manual device you control moment-by-moment into a reliable production tool that your operator can manage with simple preset recalls. A properly designed preset system removes the need for manual camera adjustment during service and allows the operator to focus on timing and responses rather than on hunting for the right camera framing.

The VHD30U typically supports multiple preset positions (often 10 to 20 depending on the model) that can be saved and recalled using the included remote control. This guide covers designing your preset system, saving presets correctly, and training your operator to use them effectively.

## Understand Your Service Flow

Before saving a single preset, understand when the camera needs to move during your service. Walk through your service order and identify the distinct moments: worship begins, teaching begins, special music, announcements, prayer. These moments define your preset system.

Most churches need four to six presets: a wide shot of the full stage, a pulpit or speaker shot, a worship team shot, possibly a choir position, and maybe a special position for announcements or prayer. Do not create more presets than you actually use. A focused set of five presets is more useful than a library of fifteen that confuses the operator.

## Save Your First Wide Preset

The wide shot is your safety position—the shot you return to if anything goes wrong. Position the camera to show the full stage, the entire worship team, and enough context for online viewers. This is usually a position with minimal zoom or moderate zoom.

Move the camera using the remote control to this position. Once the framing looks right, use the Preset Save function on the remote. Your camera documentation will specify the exact button sequence, but typically you press a Menu or Settings button, navigate to Presets, select a preset number (like Preset 1), and press Save.

Test the preset by moving the camera away from the saved position and then recalling Preset 1. The camera should return to the exact same position, zoom level, and pan/tilt angle. If it does, the preset is working correctly. If the camera does not return to the exact position, adjust the framing and resave the preset.

## Create Speaker or Pulpit Preset

Your second preset should frame the primary speaker or pulpit. Position the camera to show the speaker's shoulders and head clearly, with adequate space above. The zoom should be moderate—close enough to see facial expression, but not so tight that it feels claustrophobic.

Position the camera carefully to this framing. Ask your speaker if the framing feels good from their perspective. Zoom that feels right to you behind the camera may feel different to the person being photographed. Once the framing is approved, save this as Preset 2 and label it Pulpit or Speaker.

This preset deserves extra attention because it is probably your most-used shot. If the framing is uncomfortable for your speaker or does not clearly show their expression to online viewers, the preset will feel wrong every week.

## Add Worship and Other Key Positions

Save a Preset 3 for worship or music moments. This might be a tight shot of the worship leader, a wide shot of the band, or something in between depending on your theology and preferences. Save it with a name like Worship or Band.

If your service includes other distinctive moments, create presets for them. You might have a Preset 4 for choir, a Preset 5 for announcements, or a Preset 6 for special moments. But again, every preset should serve a clear purpose in your service. Do not create presets just because you can.

## Document Your Preset System Clearly

Once you have saved your presets, create a simple reference sheet that maps preset numbers to service moments and describes what each preset shows. Your reference might look like:

Preset 1: Wide — Full stage, entire team visible, 10x zoom
Preset 2: Pulpit — Speaker close-up, 18x zoom, shoulders to head
Preset 3: Worship — Lead singer tight, 22x zoom
Preset 4: Choir — Choir from audience perspective, 8x zoom

Print this reference and tape it near your camera control device or post it on your production computer where the operator can see it every Sunday. This eliminates ambiguity: the operator knows exactly what each preset is designed to show.

## Test Presets During Full Service Rehearsal

Before Sunday, run through your complete service order using your presets. Start with Preset 1 (Wide), move to Preset 2 (Pulpit) when teaching begins, transition to Preset 3 (Worship) when worship starts, and cycle back. Time these transitions and feel how natural they are.

If every transition requires a fast, jerky camera movement, consider adjusting your presets so movements are less dramatic. A slightly different framing might mean smoother transitions that feel more professional to online viewers.

Also test your backup position. If something goes wrong with the preset system mid-service, your operator should default to Preset 1 (Wide) and operate manually if needed. Make sure this fallback has been practiced.

## Train Your Operator to Use Presets Reliably

Successful preset operation depends on operator training and discipline. Walk your operator through each preset, showing them what each one displays and when in the service it is used. Have them practice recalling presets several times until the motion becomes automatic.

Create a simple operator runsheet that shows the service order with preset recalls alongside. For example:

Worship begins → Preset 3
Teacher walks to pulpit → Preset 2
Worship team steps back → Preset 1
Announcement → Preset 4
Closing prayer → Preset 1 (Wide)

This runsheet becomes your operator's script. They follow it, recalling presets at the right moment. The focus is on timing, not on finding the right camera position.

## Avoid Over-Relying on Manual Control

If your operator is manually controlling the camera (panning, tilting, zooming) more than half the time, your preset system needs work. Effective preset systems mean the operator uses manual control rarely and mostly uses preset recalls.

If your operator complains that they need manual control to get the right shots, you likely need more presets or need to adjust your existing presets to better match your service flow.

## Update Presets When Service Flow Changes

Your preset system is not permanent. When your service changes (seasonal variations, new liturgy, room reconfiguration), update the presets accordingly. Review your presets quarterly or whenever your service changes significantly.

To update a preset, position the camera at the new desired position and resave the preset using the same preset number. The camera overwrites the old preset with the new position.

## Manage Preset Numbering Across Multiple Services

If you run multiple services (early and late services), you might want consistent preset numbering across all services. This eliminates confusion: Preset 1 is always Wide regardless of which service is running.

Alternatively, if your services differ significantly in format, you might assign different preset numbering to each service and clearly label which preset numbers apply to which service. Document this carefully so there is no confusion.

## Conclusion

The Tenveo VHD30U's preset system is a powerful tool for reducing operator load and creating consistent, professional camera work. A well-designed preset system removes guesswork from your operator's job and lets them focus on the one thing that matters: responding to what is happening in the service and timing preset recalls appropriately.

Invest time early in designing presets that match your actual service flow, naming them clearly, and training your operator. That investment pays dividends week after week as your operator gains confidence and skill.
