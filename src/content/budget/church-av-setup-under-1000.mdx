## The $1,000 Sweet Spot

At $1,000, you're entering the realm of legitimate church streaming. You have quality components that will serve your congregation well for years. You're not cutting corners on essential elements. You're not choosing between good audio and good video—you're getting both.

This budget tier is perfect for churches with 50-200 attendees that are serious about streaming. You're saying "streaming is part of our ministry" and backing that up with equipment that works reliably and looks professional.

## Video Capture: The AVKANS PTZ

At this tier, the centerpiece is a quality PTZ camera. The AVKANS U100 costs around $450 and is the best bang for buck in church AV. It's NDI-native (connects directly to your network with one Ethernet cable carrying power and video), has a 20x optical zoom, and includes a joystick controller for smooth pan-tilt-zoom operation.

This single camera can produce an entire service without a second camera operator. Using the joystick, you can frame wide shots of your entire stage, medium shots of your worship leader, and tight shots of your pastor's face. One trained operator controls all of this smoothly.

The camera's network connectivity is crucial at this budget. Instead of running separate power cables and HDMI runs across your sanctuary, a single CAT-6 Ethernet cable carries everything. This simplifies cable management and reduces points of failure.

## Switching: ATEM Mini

The ATEM Mini is the standard-bearer for budget switching. At around $400, it gives you professional switching capabilities that a church can actually use.

The ATEM Mini accepts four HDMI inputs. You can connect your AVKANS camera (via a small network-to-HDMI converter), your ProPresenter output, a backup camera or graphics source, and maybe a video playback device. Between these inputs, your switcher operator creates the broadcast.

The ATEM Mini includes simple, intuitive buttons for switching between inputs and executing basic transitions. Volunteers learn the interface quickly. There's no software confusion—just hardware buttons that do exactly what you expect.

One critical feature: the ATEM Mini records to a USB-connected SSD. Every service is automatically recorded in broadcast quality. You're archiving your ministry without additional complexity.

## Audio: Behringer X-Air XR12

The X-Air XR12 is a digital mixer that costs around $300 and is specifically designed for streaming churches. It has twelve channels (enough for worship leader mics, instrument inputs, playback audio, and backup inputs), built-in WiFi so you can control it remotely, and most importantly, a built-in streaming preset that sends a perfectly balanced stereo mix to your streaming PC via USB.

The XR12 connects to your network, not via cables to your switcher. This means you can position your mixing position wherever you want—it doesn't need to be near your switcher. A technical person can be mixing from a side room while your switcher operator is in the main control area.

The "streaming out" function on the XR12 is preconfigured to send a stereo mix optimized for online broadcast. You don't have to think about levels or routing—the built-in preset handles it.

## Streaming Software and PC

OBS (free) is your streaming platform. It's powerful enough for this setup and has the advantage of being free and open-source. Your streaming PC receives video from the ATEM Mini (via HDMI capture card, $30) and audio from the X-Air XR12 (via USB), combines them, and streams to YouTube and Facebook.

Your PC doesn't need to be fancy. A mid-range laptop with an Intel i5 processor and a basic GPU handles 1080p streaming at 30fps without struggling. You're not editing video on this machine—just capturing and encoding a live stream.

## Total Budget at Under $1,000

- AVKANS PTZ Camera: $450
- ATEM Mini Switcher: $400
- Behringer X-Air XR12 Mixer: $300
- HDMI Video Capture Card: $30
- Network-to-HDMI Converter (for camera): $50
- Cables and connectors: $30

Total: $1,260

Wait, that's over $1,000. Here's how to trim it: if you already have a streaming PC, you've already spent on that. If you already have decent microphones and a basic PA system, you can skip the mixer for now and use a simple USB audio interface ($80) to capture from your PA output.

With those optimizations, you get to $950-1,000 for your core streaming system.

## What This System Enables

You have one camera that a trained operator controls via joystick. They can frame wide shots, medium shots, and tight shots without a second operator. Your service looks professional because your camera work is intentional and smooth.

Your audio is professionally mixed. Vocals are balanced, music sits underneath, and your streaming audience hears what you intended them to hear—not the muffled audio from a camera microphone.

Your service is automatically recorded, so you have archives for people who missed it, for social media clips, or just for your records.

You're streaming to YouTube and Facebook simultaneously, reaching people on whatever platform they prefer.

A volunteer operator (after a few weeks of training) can run the entire operation smoothly, week after week. This is a sustainable system, not a one-person technical show.

## The Operational Reality

Your switcher operator has simple instructions: "Start with Scene 1 (your opening bumper). When worship starts, press Camera 1. Adjust framing with the joystick. When announcements start, press Slide 2 (your ProPresenter output). When teaching starts, press Camera 1 again."

Your audio engineer has preset mix levels: worship songs should have vocals at -12dB, speaking should be at -6dB. If something sounds wrong, adjust the fader for that input. Most of the time, the preset handles everything.

You click "Start Streaming" when service begins and "Stop Streaming" when it ends. That's your entire technical burden.

## Scaling From Here

After six months with this setup, you'll understand where to invest next. Maybe you'll add a second PTZ camera for more coverage. Maybe you'll upgrade to an ATEM Mini Pro for better recording and more inputs. Maybe you'll upgrade the XR12 to an X32 for more channels and deeper mixing capabilities.

The foundation is solid. Everything is professional-grade. You're not limited by cheap components. You're just building experience and deciding what to add based on real Sunday morning feedback.

The $1,000 investment hits a sweet spot where you're getting professional results without breaking the budget. The AVKANS is a real PTZ camera (not a toy). The ATEM Mini is the switcher that professional churches use. The XR12 is a real digital mixer, not a USB preamp.

Every component is legitimate. Every component will serve your church reliably for years. You're not compromising on the things that matter (audio quality, video framing, mixing capability). You're just not paying for features you don't need yet.
