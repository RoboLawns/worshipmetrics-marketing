# Multi-Track Recording on the Behringer X32

One of the most valuable capabilities of the Behringer X32 is its ability to send up to 32 individual audio channels simultaneously to a computer over USB, enabling true multi-track recording. Instead of recording just a stereo mix like you would with a handheld recorder, you can capture every microphone, instrument, and bus as its own separate file. This opens up profound possibilities for your church—sermons can be distributed with professional podcast-quality audio, the worship team can review their performance with perfect clarity, and you have the raw material for professional video production.

The process might sound technically complex, but it's surprisingly straightforward once you understand the basic concepts. The X32's USB interface sends the audio, your computer's digital audio workstation (DAW) receives it, and you hit record. Afterwards, you have a complete archive of your service ready for editing, mixing, or distribution.

## Understanding Multi-Track USB Recording

When you connect the X32 to a computer via USB, the mixer becomes a sophisticated audio interface capable of sending up to 32 separate channels of audio simultaneously. Your DAW—software like Reaper, Adobe Audition, or even free options like Audacity—can be configured to receive all these channels at once and record them to a multi-track project file.

The beauty of multi-track recording is flexibility. During the live service, you're mixing in real-time for your congregation, creating the best possible experience for people in the room. Later, you can take that raw multi-track recording and remix it for podcast, apply professional mastering, or extract the sermon with perfect audio quality. The worship leader can listen to a perfect recording of their vocal and instruments to review their performance. The liturgist can extract their speaking voice perfectly isolated from background music.

The technical setup requires your X32 to be configured with USB audio enabled, a computer with sufficient processing power and disk space, and a DAW capable of recording 32 simultaneous tracks. Most modern computers meet these requirements, though recording 32 tracks of 48 kHz, 24-bit audio does generate substantial disk usage—approximately 5 GB per hour for all 32 channels.

## Configuring USB Audio on the X32

First, ensure your X32 has USB audio enabled. Navigate to Setup on the mixer, then IO. Look for the USB section. Enable "USB Audio" if it's not already activated. The X32 will now appear as an audio device on your computer when connected via USB cable.

Next, you need to configure which channels get sent over USB. Go back to the IO menu and locate the USB Send section. This is where you decide whether to send the main stereo mix, individual channels, or a combination. For multi-track recording, you'll want to set this to "All Channels" or configure it to send your primary input channels plus any buses you want to capture.

The USB interface can send up to 32 channels, but you don't necessarily need all of them. If you only use 16 input channels in your setup, configure the USB to send those 16 channels plus perhaps your main stereo output and your streaming auxiliary bus. This reduces the disk space requirements and keeps your DAW project manageable.

## Selecting and Setting Up Your DAW

Reaper is the most popular choice for church multi-track recording because it's affordable, incredibly flexible, and handles large sessions with ease. Download a free 60-day trial from cockos.com. Other solid options include Pro Tools, Logic Pro, or the free Ardour.

Open your DAW and create a new project. Set the sample rate to 48 kHz and bit depth to 24 bits, matching the X32's output. These settings ensure the highest audio quality for your archive. Set your recording location to a hard drive with sufficient free space—plan for at least 50 GB if you're going to record regular services.

Now, configure your audio inputs. In your DAW's preferences or settings, set the default audio input to "Behringer X32" (or whatever your system labels the mixer). Create a new track for each of your input channels. If the X32 is sending 16 channels, create 16 tracks. Label each track with its channel name—"Lead Vocal," "Kick Drum," "Guitar 1," and so on.

## Setting Input Levels in Your DAW

Each track in your DAW has its own input level adjustment. These should generally be set to unity gain (0 dB) initially, allowing the channel's level as set on the X32 to pass through unchanged. However, if you notice that the DAW's input meters are showing peaks above -6 dB, you may want to reduce the track input level slightly to ensure you have enough headroom in your recording.

The goal during recording is to capture strong, clean signal without clipping. Your DAW's meters should show peaks around -12 dB to -6 dB during the service. If you see peaks regularly hitting red, the input level is too high and you risk distorted recordings. If peaks are barely visible at -24 dB or quieter, the input level is too low and you'll have a noisy recording. Adjust the DAW's track input level to get the signal into the right zone.

## The Multi-Track Recording Process

Before the service begins, open your multi-track project in the DAW, verify that all inputs are properly routed, and arm the project for recording. This usually means clicking a record button or pressing a keyboard shortcut. The DAW is now ready to capture audio.

When service starts, hit the record button. The DAW begins capturing all your input channels simultaneously. The mixer is still operating normally—your audio engineer is mixing for the congregation using the X32's faders and controls. The recording captures that raw audio feed without processing, so you can remix it later if desired.

During the service, periodically glance at your DAW's metering to ensure recording is happening normally. Confirm that you're seeing activity on all the channels you expect to record. If a channel is completely silent, it might not be routed properly.

After the service ends, stop recording in the DAW. Your DAW now contains a complete multi-track project with the entire service captured. This recording is your archive.

## Post-Recording Workflow

The raw multi-track recording from the service is just the starting point. Most churches do some post-processing. At minimum, you'll want to export a stereo mix. Open your DAW's mixer and adjust the levels of each track to create a good-sounding stereo file. This becomes your "master recording" of the service.

For sermon podcast, you might solo the vocal microphone channel and any background music. Edit out any dead air, add a small amount of compression to even out level variations, and you have professional podcast audio. This takes 30-60 minutes of work per week but results in significantly better sermon distribution than audio recorded from the room.

For worship team review, export stems—separate stereo files for drums, vocals, guitars, keyboards, and so on. Send these to band members so they can listen to their own performance in context.

## Storage and Archival Considerations

Multi-track recordings consume significant disk space. A typical hour-long service with 16 active channels will be approximately 3 GB. You'll want a systematic archival strategy. Many churches maintain a local backup drive and cloud storage. Services are stored with clear date naming—"2024-04-14-Worship-Service.reaper" and related audio files. This allows you to quickly locate any service for review, remixing, or content extraction.

## Troubleshooting Multi-Track Recording Issues

**No audio or incomplete audio recorded**: Verify that the X32 is set to send audio over USB and that all your input channels are routed to the USB send. Check your DAW to confirm the audio input is set to the X32. Make sure the record button is actually armed in your DAW.

**Clipping or distorted recording**: The input level from the X32 is too high. Either reduce the preamp gain on the X32 for that channel, or reduce the track input level in the DAW. Aim for -12 dB to -6 dB peaks on the DAW meters.

**Out of sync audio**: If your recording sounds like it's slowly drifting out of time with itself (one channel's vocals gradually falling behind the instruments), it's a sample rate mismatch. Verify that your DAW's sample rate matches the X32's sample rate—both should be 48 kHz.

**Project keeps dropping audio**: You're likely exceeding your computer's processing capacity or your hard drive can't write data fast enough. Close other applications, move your project to a faster hard drive (SSD if possible), and reduce the number of simultaneous tracks you're recording.

## Expanding Your Multi-Track Capabilities

Once you're comfortable with basic multi-track recording, consider adding video synchronization. Record video of your service simultaneously with your audio multi-track. Later, you can sync them in editing software, allowing you to create professional worship recordings with full video and pristine audio. Many churches use this for creating YouTube worship videos or providing full service recordings for their website.

Another expansion is recording the main stereo mix simultaneously with multi-track. This gives you your safety master recording plus the flexibility of individual channels. If you realize later that you need a perfect vocal-only version, you have it. If the multi-track recording has an issue, you still have the stereo backup.

Multi-track recording transforms the X32 from a live mixing tool into a complete production studio. Your services are now permanently documented at the highest possible quality, ready for any future purpose.
