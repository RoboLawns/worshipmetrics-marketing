# Allen & Heath SQ-6 Setup Guide for Larger Churches

The Allen & Heath SQ-6 represents the flagship of the SQ mixer line, engineered specifically for larger facilities, complex productions, and churches that demand more channel capacity than the SQ-5 can provide. With support for more simultaneous inputs, greater processing power, and expanded networked capability, the SQ-6 handles the most demanding worship audio scenarios without strain. Understanding how to properly configure this powerful console ensures your larger church facility gets the performance and reliability it needs.

## Assessing Your SQ-6 vs SQ-5 Decision

Before diving into setup, confirm that the SQ-6 is the right choice for your facility. The primary advantage of the SQ-6 is expanded channel capacity and processing horsepower. If your church runs large worship bands with multiple keyboards, additional background vocalists, live drums, and a full sound design with effects, the SQ-6 makes sense. If you're running simpler worship setups, the SQ-5 likely serves you perfectly well.

The SQ-6 also provides more auxiliary buses for monitor mixing, effects sends, and additional broadcast outputs. Larger churches with multiple zones, outdoor overflow areas, or complex stage monitor requirements benefit significantly from this expanded flexibility. Additionally, the SQ-6 processes audio with greater processing capacity, meaning you can add comprehensive EQ and dynamics processing to more simultaneous channels without the mixer reaching processing limits.

## Physical Connection and Power Considerations

The SQ-6 is substantially larger than the SQ-5, requiring dedicated counter space or console furniture in your control room. Before purchasing and installing the mixer, measure your proposed location carefully and ensure adequate ventilation. The SQ-6 generates more heat than smaller consoles and requires airflow around its rear panel.

Connect the SQ-6 to a quality power conditioner rather than directly to the wall outlet. This protects the console from power surges and provides clean, stable power critical to audio quality. Allow at least 30 minutes of warm-up time before your first service, giving the mixer's internal circuits time to stabilize.

## Configuring Your Input Infrastructure

The SQ-6 provides more physical analog input connectors than the SQ-5, and additional expansion is possible through digital networking. Begin by mapping out your church's audio sources: microphones, instruments, playback devices, and network audio. Create a diagram showing which source feeds which SQ-6 input, and use this diagram when training volunteers and troubleshooting problems.

Connect your wireless microphone receivers first, using the same gain-setting approach as smaller consoles. Bring each source to approximately -12 dB peak level on the input meter. Do this for every input you plan to use regularly, not just your Sunday sources—establish good practices that protect your entire mixing infrastructure.

The SQ-6 handles both balanced XLR and quarter-inch phone connectors, depending on your source equipment. Most professional sources use balanced XLR; use this whenever available. Unbalanced connections are acceptable for short cable runs, but longer cable runs benefit from balanced connectivity to minimize noise and interference.

## Building Your Master Mix Infrastructure

With inputs configured, establish your primary mixing path. The SQ-6's mixing architecture is similar to the SQ-5, but with expanded capacity. Touch the Outputs section and configure your stereo main output. This is your front-of-house mix that the congregation hears through your main speakers.

Assign your configured inputs to a primary mix bus—typically called MIX1 on the SQ-6. Unlike smaller consoles, the SQ-6 can handle significantly more channels in a single mix without CPU limitation. However, good mixing practice suggests organization regardless of technical capacity. Group your sources logically: lead vocals, supporting vocals, drums, instruments, and ambient audio.

Set reasonable starting levels for each channel feeding your main mix. The goal at this stage is simply to establish that signal is flowing from inputs through the main output correctly. Refinement and balancing come later during rehearsal and service preparation.

## Utilizing Expanded Monitor Capability

Larger churches often need multiple monitor mixes for the band, choir, or worship leaders. The SQ-6's expanded auxiliary bus infrastructure makes this straightforward. Create separate auxiliary mixes for stage monitors, choir monitors, and perhaps an overflow room monitor feed.

Configure your first auxiliary mix—AUX1—as a stage monitor mix. This will be fed to a monitor amplifier and speakers positioned near your band or worship leaders. Assign channels to this auxiliary just as you would the main mix, but customize the level of each source to what stage performers want to hear.

Create a second auxiliary for the choir or additional worship team members. Adjust the mix carefully so that performers hear exactly what they need to perform well together. The ability to provide multiple custom mixes simultaneously is one of the SQ-6's most powerful features for worship contexts.

## Advanced Networking: Adding Digital Audio Infrastructure

The SQ-6 supports comprehensive networking through both dSNAKE and GigACE protocols. If you're deploying multiple stage boxes, wireless monitoring systems, or other networked audio equipment, now is the time to configure this infrastructure.

Navigate to the System Settings and access the Network section. Configure the Ethernet ports according to your facility's specific topology. Some churches use a dedicated network for AV equipment separate from their guest WiFi and office network. This segregation improves reliability and security.

If you're adding remote control capability through iPad, configure the WiFi settings now. The SQ-6 can create its own WiFi network for control tablets, or connect to your existing facility network. Most larger churches benefit from a dedicated WiFi network for mixing control to minimize interference with other wireless systems.

## Establishing Scene Structure for Complex Services

With the SQ-6's processing capacity, you can create remarkably detailed scenes for different service elements. Instead of generic scenes, create highly specific scenes: "Worship Set 1 - Opening," "Sermon Portion 1," "Communion," "Worship Set 2 - Closing," each optimized for that exact portion of your service.

Because the SQ-6 has significant processing headroom, you can apply comprehensive effects processing, multiple bus compression, and detailed EQ to numerous channels simultaneously. Build your scenes with this capacity in mind, creating sophisticated processing chains that support your aesthetic preferences and room acoustics.

## Backup and Redundancy Planning

For larger churches, having backup mixing capability is essential. If your SQ-6 fails, what's your contingency? Some churches maintain a secondary mixer—perhaps a smaller SQ-5—as a fallback. Others maintain USB-backed-up show files that can be restored to a replacement mixer quickly if needed.

At minimum, export your show files weekly to a USB drive kept in a secure location. Export these regularly throughout the year, not just after making changes. This practice ensures that if hardware failure occurs, you can restore your shows quickly rather than rebuilding them from memory.

## Commissioning Your SQ-6 System

Before trusting the SQ-6 with your first live service, spend substantial time in rehearsal becoming familiar with its layout and workflow. Your entire mixing team should participate in this commissioning process, not just your primary operator. Have backup operators practice recalling scenes, adjusting monitor mixes, and handling the most common mixing tasks.

During commissioning rehearsals, intentionally try to break things—mute channels by accident, adjust processing, change routing. This builds your team's confidence that mistakes are recoverable and that the mixer's protection features work as advertised. An experienced team that has practiced recovery scenarios is vastly more confident during actual services.

Your Allen & Heath SQ-6 now provides the expanded capacity and sophisticated capabilities that larger churches demand. Treat it as the centerpiece of your worship audio infrastructure, and it will serve your ministry faithfully for many years.
