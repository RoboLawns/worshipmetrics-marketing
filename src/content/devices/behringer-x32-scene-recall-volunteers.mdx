# Behringer X32 Scene Recall: Building a Volunteer-Friendly Setup

One of the most powerful features of the Behringer X32 is its ability to save and recall scenes. A scene captures every setting on the mixer at a specific moment—all your fader positions, channel names, EQ settings, effects, routing decisions, and auxiliary levels. For churches that rely on volunteers to operate their audio, scenes are transformative. Instead of asking a volunteer to manually set up 32 channels, adjust all their levels, and configure monitors, you simply press a button and the entire mixer reverts to your saved configuration. This reliability is why many successful church tech teams build their entire workflow around scenes.

## What Scenes Actually Save

When you save a scene on the X32, the mixer records the complete state of nearly every parameter. This includes the position of every fader, the setting of every button, the name of every channel, all your EQ and compression settings, your effects settings, your DCA (Dynamics Control Assign) groups if you're using them, and your routing configuration. The exception is that scenes do not save file names or show structure—those are stored separately as part of a "show file," which is a container that holds multiple scenes plus other settings.

Understanding this distinction is important. You might save a scene called "Sunday Service Starting Mix" that has all your channels dialed in beautifully. When you recall that scene, the X32 instantly returns every single parameter to match that snapshot. A volunteer doesn't need to remember that the lead vocal should be at -3 dB or that the drums need 4 dB of reverb. Those settings are locked in the scene.

## Building Your First Full Service Scene

The best approach is to plan your first full service scene during a rehearsal or sound check. Bring in your worship band and have them play through your typical service sequence. Go through your normal mixing process, dialing in the lead vocal, balancing the drums, adding any effects you typically use, and getting the overall mix sounding great.

As you do this, you should have named all your channels appropriately. Channel 1 might be "Lead Vocal," Channel 2 "Acoustic Guitar," Channel 3 "Electric Guitar," and so on. These names persist across all scenes once you've set them, which helps anyone using the mixer understand what each channel controls. Use clear, descriptive names that would mean something to a substitute volunteer who's never used your specific setup.

Once the mix sounds good, you're ready to save the scene. Press the Scene button on the X32 to access the scene menu. Select "New Scene" and give it a descriptive name like "Sunday Service Mix." You can add a description if you want additional notes. Press "Save" and the mixer stores this complete snapshot.

## Creating Variations for Different Service Elements

Many churches benefit from having multiple scenes within a single service. Your worship set might use one mix, your announcements section might benefit from a different mix with the vocal higher and music lower, and your sermon might use a completely different configuration with no backing track. You can save separate scenes for each section.

To create these variations, recall your main service scene first. Then make adjustments for your announcements section—perhaps pull down the music bus and bring the vocal up. Save this as "Sunday Service Announcements." Later, adjust again for sermon—maybe turn off the music entirely and raise the vocal slightly. Save as "Sunday Service Sermon." Now you have three separate snapshots, and your volunteer operator can seamlessly transition between them just by pressing one button.

## Show Files Versus Individual Scenes

The X32 organizes scenes into "show files." Think of a show file as a folder containing all your scenes for a particular type of event. Your "Sunday Service" show file might contain ten different scenes covering every element of your typical Sunday. Your "Wedding Reception" show file might have different scenes. Your "Special Event" show file would have yet another set.

When you power on the X32, it loads the last show file and scene you had open. This is incredibly useful for consistency. If you always open your "Sunday Service" show file on Sunday morning and select your opening worship scene, the mixer will be exactly where you left it the previous Sunday.

To access show files, press Setup on the mixer, then navigate to Show. Here you can create new shows, rename existing ones, and manage your collection. Most churches keep just one or two show files in regular rotation—perhaps one for typical Sundays and one for special events or holidays with different music.

## Protecting Your Settings from Accidental Changes

One of the biggest fears of church tech teams is a volunteer accidentally changing something they shouldn't. Fortunately, the X32 offers several protective mechanisms. The most basic is simply making sure volunteers understand to only touch the faders and nothing else. Most audio decisions should be locked into scenes, so they don't need to adjust EQ, effects, or routing.

More advanced protection comes from the X32's ability to lock certain parameters. Go to Setup and look for a "Scene Protect" option (availability depends on your firmware version). This allows you to specify exactly which parameters get saved and recalled with scenes. You might, for example, set it to save and recall fader positions and channel routing, but not allow changes to EQ settings. When a volunteer loads the scene, they get the right fader positions but can't accidentally alter your carefully crafted EQ.

Another layer of protection is physical. If you're operating remotely or don't want any fader changes at all, you can use the X32-Edit software running on a tablet or laptop to control the mixer remotely. Only your designated engineer has access to the tablet, and volunteers stick to triggering scenes from the physical console.

## Training Volunteers to Load Scenes

The beauty of scenes is that loading them requires minimal training. A volunteer needs to understand just two steps: First, at the beginning of service, open the correct show file if it's not already open. Second, select the first scene (your opening worship scene). That's it. The rest of the service is handled with scene recalls triggered at appropriate moments by someone who understands the flow.

You can write these instructions on a simple printed card that sits next to the mixer: "1. Check that 'Sunday Service' show is selected at the top of the screen. 2. Select 'Opening Worship' scene before service begins." Once they've done this a few times, it becomes automatic.

For additional safety, you might designate one person—your primary audio engineer—as responsible for all scene selections during the service. Volunteers can operate the main faders but don't have permission to change scenes. This centralizes control and reduces the chance of mistakes.

## Managing Scene Backups

Scenes are stored on the X32's internal memory, but it's important to have backups. The X32 can export show files to a USB drive. This is critical. Before you rely heavily on scenes, plug a USB drive into the mixer and export your show file. Keep multiple copies of these backups. If something corrupts the internal memory (unlikely but possible), you can quickly reload your show files and get back to normal.

Export show files regularly, especially after adding new scenes or making significant changes. Think of this as part of your Sunday routine—after service, export the current show file to an updated backup drive.

## Leveraging Scenes for Consistency and Reliability

When scenes are set up correctly, your church's audio production becomes dramatically more reliable. Every Sunday at the same point in the service, the mix sounds the same. Your volunteers don't need deep mixing knowledge. Your guest sound engineers can quickly understand the structure by looking at scene names. Backups mean you never lose your work.

Start simple with just one basic scene, use it for a few weeks, and build confidence. Then start adding variations. As you become more comfortable, you'll find that scenes let you focus on the creative side of mixing—making small tweaks to the opening song mix or adding a delay effect to the prayer response—rather than constantly managing basic setup tasks.
