# Behringer X-Air XR12 Setup for Small Churches

The Behringer XR12 is the most compact and affordable option in the X-Air family, designed specifically for small venues, churches, and portable applications. With 12 input channels and a rackmount form factor, the XR12 delivers professional mixing capability at an entry-level price point. It's controlled entirely through the X Air app on tablets, making it accessible to volunteers while maintaining professional audio quality.

## Understanding the XR12's Design

The XR12 is a 12-channel, networked digital mixer in a compact 1U rackmount enclosure. Like the XR18, it has no physical control surface. All mixing happens through the X Air app on iPads or Android tablets. The benefit is that you get professional mixing features without the cost and space demands of a physical console.

For very small churches with 6-10 active microphones and instruments, the XR12 provides all the capability you need. Twelve channels are plenty for a small worship team. The mixer handles gain staging, effects, EQ, and mixing just like larger Behringer mixers.

The XR12 is also extremely portable. At roughly the size of a small router, you can carry it between rooms, multiple campuses, or home for software updates. This makes it ideal for churches with satellite locations or frequently changing tech spaces.

## Physical Setup

Unpack the XR12 and position it either in a standard 19-inch equipment rack using the included rackmount ears, or simply on a shelf or table next to your power amplifier. It requires no special mounting.

Connect your audio cables to the rear panel. You have 12 XLR microphone inputs on the back, along with analog outputs for your main L/R mix and a few auxiliary outputs. Connect these the same way you would on any mixer—microphones to inputs, main output to your power amplifier.

Plug the included power supply into a standard wall outlet. The XR12 powers on automatically. Look at the front panel for indicator lights showing power and network status. Unlike larger consoles, there's no screen, so you rely entirely on these status lights and the X Air app.

## Network Configuration

The XR12 connects to your network via Wi-Fi or Ethernet, just like the XR18. For reliability, use Ethernet if possible. Run an Ethernet cable from your network switch to the XR12's rear panel Ethernet port. The mixer immediately connects to your network.

If Ethernet isn't practical, enable Wi-Fi. Access the XR12's network setup menu by pressing the small Setup button on the front panel. Navigate to Network settings and select your church's Wi-Fi network. Enter the password and the mixer connects wirelessly.

## X Air App Installation and Connection

Download the free X Air app from the App Store (iPad) or Google Play (Android). Launch the app and it automatically searches your network for connected XR12 mixers. When it finds yours, tap on it to connect.

Once connected, the X Air interface displays all 12 channels with their faders, metering, and controls. Unlike the physical consoles of larger mixers, everything you do happens by dragging faders on the screen, tapping buttons, and adjusting parameters through touch.

## Basic Configuration

Start by naming your 12 channels. Open each channel's settings and give it a clear name: "Lead Vocal," "Acoustic Guitar," "Kick Drum," "Keys," and so on. This makes operation intuitive for volunteers.

Set the gain (input level) for each channel. As someone speaks or plays into a microphone, watch the input meter. Adjust gain so that typical peaks sit around -12 dB. This is proper gain staging.

Verify that all your active channels are routed to the main output (LR). In the X Air app, check the routing for each channel. Set the main output faders to approximately 0 dB unity gain.

## Simplifying the XR12 for Volunteers

Because the XR12 is operating a smaller number of channels, it's easier to train volunteers. Create a simplified X Air layout showing only the channels you actually use. For a 7-piece worship team, you only need 7 channels visible. Hide channels 8-12 so there's less visual clutter.

Volunteers learn a streamlined interface rather than being overwhelmed by controls they don't need. This accelerates training and reduces mixing mistakes.

## Mixing Workflow

Unlike operating a physical console where you're constantly adjusting many faders simultaneously, the XR12 workflow is more deliberate. You're working with a tablet, dragging digital faders, and making parameter changes through taps and touches.

This works beautifully for planned services where most of your levels are set before the service through scenes (covered in detail in other guides). During the service, you make occasional adjustments: bringing a vocal up slightly, reducing drum level during a prayer response, adding a touch of reverb to a spoken prayer.

## Effects and Processing

The XR12 includes professional effects and EQ just like its larger siblings. In the X Air app, access the effects section and add reverb, delay, compression, and other processing. For worship, a subtle reverb on the lead vocal and maybe some gentle compression on the drums is typical.

Set these up during rehearsal and save them with your scene. Every Sunday you load the scene and the effects are already configured.

## Scene-Based Operations

Use scenes to save your complete setup. After you've configured all 12 channels, set levels, added effects, and tested everything, save a scene called "Sunday Worship." Next week, load this scene and you're 90% of the way to perfect settings.

Create variations for different service elements if your church changes the mix between worship, announcements, and sermon. But for most small churches, one good scene covers the entire service.

## Expandability and Limitations

The XR12 maxes out at 12 input channels. If your church grows to consistently need more than 12 simultaneous inputs, you'd need to upgrade to the XR18 or larger. However, 12 channels covers most small churches' needs.

The XR12 doesn't support Dante networking or complex multi-room setups. For simple single-room church mixing, this isn't a limitation. The XR12 does support USB audio and basic recording functionality.

## Getting Started This Week

Week one: Mount or position the XR12. Connect all audio cables. Power it on. Download X Air on an iPad. Connect to the network and pair with the mixer. Name your channels. Test audio flow to your speakers.

Week two: Do a complete sound check, setting gain on all channels and testing the mix. Create your first scene.

Week three: Train a volunteer to load the scene and operate basic faders. Refine the scene based on what you learn.

By week four, you're mixing services comfortably on the XR12. The learning curve is gentle because there's nothing physically complicated about the hardware.

## The XR12 for Growing Churches

Many churches start with the XR12 because of its affordability and simplicity. As they grow, if they consistently need more than 12 channels, they upgrade to the XR18. Your XR12 doesn't become useless—you might repurpose it for a satellite campus, youth group, or coffee shop service.

Alternatively, many churches stay with the XR12 long-term because 12 channels is genuinely all they need. There's no requirement to eventually upgrade.

## The XR12's Strength

The XR12's strength is value. You get professional Behringer mixing engine at an entry-level price. You get tablet control flexibility. You avoid the cost and complexity of a physical console. For cash-strapped churches just getting started with digital mixing, the XR12 is transformative—it opens up professional capabilities that would otherwise require expensive hardware.

Welcome to wireless mixing. The XR12 is ready to serve your worship for years to come.
