# Allen & Heath CQ-18T vs Behringer XR18 for Churches

For small churches taking their first steps into digital mixing, two compact tablets-based mixers dominate: the Allen & Heath CQ-18T and the Behringer XR18. Both represent significant value in the sub-$500 price range and have proven reliability in countless installations. Understanding their differences helps you choose the right console for your church's specific situation and mixing philosophy.

## Physical Design and Interface Philosophy

The Allen & Heath CQ-18T embraces a touchscreen-first design. The entire interface is capacitive touchscreen with minimal physical controls. All mixing happens through tapping, dragging, and swiping on the seven-inch display. This minimalist approach results in a compact, portable mixer that fits easily into any control space.

The Behringer XR18 takes a different approach, providing a larger color touchscreen combined with more physical controls—physical faders for the eight main channels, navigation buttons, and a control surface that bridges the gap between purely digital and traditional analog mixing. The result is a slightly larger console that provides more immediate tactile feedback.

For churches with limited space, the CQ-18T's smaller footprint is genuinely valuable. For operators preferring tactile control and seeing more information simultaneously, the XR18's larger design might feel more natural.

## Mixing Architecture and Channel Count

Both mixers provide 18 channels of mixing capability. The CQ-18T provides six stereo input pairs. The XR18 similarly provides 18 channels. In practical terms, both handle the mixing requirements of small churches equally well.

The XR18 includes slightly more processing headroom and mixing flexibility, but for small-church applications where you're likely using 8-12 of 18 available channels, both consoles provide more than adequate capacity.

## Processing and Effects

The CQ-18T includes basic built-in effects: reverb, delay, and chorus. These effects are adequate for simple worship setups. On-channel compression and EQ provide fundamental processing without excessive complexity.

The Behringer XR18 provides more extensive effects and processing options. More reverb types, more simultaneous effects, and more on-channel processing tools are available. If your worship aesthetic involves sophisticated effects stacking, the XR18 provides more capability.

For most small churches with straightforward mixing needs, the CQ-18T's processing is sufficient. As your technical sophistication grows, you might eventually wish for more processing options—a potential future upgrade incentive if your church outgrows initial needs.

## App Control and Remote Mixing

The CQ-18T works with the Allen & Heath online Control app, providing iPad and iPhone remote control. The app connects via WiFi or local network, enabling mixing from anywhere with connectivity.

The Behringer XR18 works with Behringer's X-AIR app for remote control. Functionally, both remote control systems work similarly, providing full mixing access from a mobile device.

Both approaches work well. Choose based on your preference for interface design and your existing familiarity with each manufacturer's products.

## Built-In WiFi Networking

The CQ-18T can create its own WiFi network, enabling direct iPad-to-mixer connectivity without requiring your facility's WiFi infrastructure. This approach eliminates network complexity and provides rock-solid connectivity independent of facility WiFi quality.

The Behringer XR18 similarly can create a dedicated WiFi network. Both manufacturers understood that reliable network connectivity is essential for wireless mixing control, so both designed self-contained WiFi capability into their consoles.

For churches where WiFi reliability is uncertain, both consoles' ability to create dedicated networks is valuable. This feature essentially guarantees mixing control reliability.

## Expandability and Growth

As your church grows, what happens if your small mixer becomes limiting? The CQ-18T integrates easily with larger Allen & Heath consoles. If you eventually upgrade to a SQ-5 or Avantis, you can use your CQ-18T for monitoring or secondary mixing alongside the larger console.

The Behringer XR18, similarly, integrates with other Behringer digital consoles. However, Behringer's expansion philosophy is somewhat different from Allen & Heath's. If you anticipate eventual expansion to significantly larger systems, the ecosystem you choose might matter.

For most small churches, however, both consoles serve for many years without needing upgrade. Don't choose based on speculative future growth—choose based on current needs.

## Price and Value Proposition

Pricing varies based on market conditions and retailer, but generally the Behringer XR18 costs slightly less than the Allen & Heath CQ-18T. For budget-constrained churches, this price difference might be meaningful. Both represent exceptional value for their price ranges.

That said, don't let $50-100 price differences drive your decision if you strongly prefer one interface or ecosystem over the other. Both consoles will serve your church well for years.

## Community and Support

The Allen & Heath CQ-18T has built a growing community of users, with increasing online resources, forums, and shared mixing templates. Allen & Heath's support for their products is generally reliable.

Behringer similarly has an enormous user community and extensive online support resources. Behringer's support is often criticized, but for something as straightforward as a small mixer, you rarely need support after initial setup.

Both consoles are popular enough that you'll find online communities, YouTube tutorials, and peer support available.

## Making Your Choice

Choose the Allen & Heath CQ-18T if: You value compact size and minimal footprint, you prefer a touchscreen-only interface, you like the simplicity of Allen & Heath's mixing philosophy, or you want straightforward functionality without feature overload.

Choose the Behringer XR18 if: You prefer more physical controls and tactile feedback, you want more extensive processing and effects options, you prefer Behringer's interface design, or you find a significantly better price on the XR18.

Honestly, either choice will serve your church excellently. Both mixers have proven themselves in countless church installations. Your choice should prioritize what feels most intuitive to you and your mixing team, not theoretical feature comparisons.

Both the Allen & Heath CQ-18T and Behringer XR18 represent outstanding value and genuine capability for small churches beginning their digital mixing journey. Whichever you choose, you're making a sound investment that will improve your worship audio while remaining affordable.
