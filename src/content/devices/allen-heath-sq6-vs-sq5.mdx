# Allen & Heath SQ-6 vs SQ-5: Which Size Does Your Church Need?

Choosing between the Allen & Heath SQ-5 and SQ-6 represents one of the most significant equipment decisions for growing churches. Both consoles share the same fundamental architecture, user interface, and reliability. The primary differences involve channel capacity, processing power, and network scalability. Understanding these differences helps you choose the right console for your current needs while anticipating your church's growth trajectory.

## Physical Size and Console Footprint

The most obvious difference between these consoles is their physical size. The SQ-5 is substantially more compact, fitting comfortably on standard control room furniture and requiring minimal space. For churches with limited control booth space or those planning portable setup for multiple services, the SQ-5's compact size is a genuine advantage.

The SQ-6 is significantly larger, requiring dedicated counter space or custom console furniture. In fixed installations where space isn't a constraint, this larger size provides tangible workflow benefits—more physical faders visible simultaneously, larger touchscreen display options, and less need for menu diving. However, if you operate your mixer from a cramped booth or move the console between services, the SQ-5's portability wins.

## Input Channel Capacity

The SQ-5 handles 24 physical analog inputs plus expansion through digital networking. For most mid-size churches running traditional worship bands with wireless vocals, instruments, and ambient coverage, 24 local inputs provide ample capacity.

The SQ-6 provides substantially more physical analog inputs, accommodating larger worship bands, complex stage setups, and churches with multiple services or worship styles. If you run large contemporary bands with multiple keyboardists, additional guitar channels, click track, band communications, and high-quality overhead drum microphones, you'll find the SQ-6's expanded local input count valuable.

The critical question: Does your current setup exceed or push against the SQ-5's 24-input limit? If you're consistently running with 22-24 of those inputs actively used, the SQ-6 provides comfortable headroom for growth. If your current setup uses only 16-20 inputs, the SQ-5 will serve you fine for years.

## Processing Power and Simultaneous Effects

Both consoles provide parametric EQ and compression per channel, but the SQ-6's processing architecture provides significantly greater simultaneous processing capacity. On the SQ-5, if you apply aggressive processing to many channels simultaneously—compression on four vocal channels, reverb on four more, parallel compression on drums—the mixer's processing power reaches capacity.

The SQ-6 handles this scenario without strain. If your worship aesthetic involves sophisticated effects chains—parallel compression, parallel reverb, complex routing—the SQ-6 provides headroom that prevents "out of processing power" messages that can occur on the SQ-5 under extreme loads.

For most churches, the SQ-5's processing capacity is entirely adequate. Simple compression on vocals, basic EQ on instruments, and a couple of reverbs on sends consume only a fraction of available processing. Only churches with very complex signal chains or multiple simultaneous effects strategies need the SQ-6's extra capacity.

## Monitor Mix Capability

Both consoles provide independent monitor mixes for stage performers. The SQ-5 includes four stereo auxiliary buses, typically sufficient for primary stage monitors plus one or two additional mixes. This works fine for churches with a single worship band and simple monitor requirements.

The SQ-6 provides significantly more auxiliary buses, enabling independent mixes for lead vocalists, backup vocalists, musicians, choir, and additional monitor zones without compromise. If you need to provide multiple radically different mixes simultaneously—the drummer needs drums and click track, the keys player needs keyboards and reference tones, the choir needs their own mix—the SQ-6 makes this feasible with dedicated outputs for each.

## Networking and Expansion

Both consoles support dSNAKE and GigACE networking. The SQ-6's larger architecture scales more gracefully to complex multi-location setups with multiple stage boxes and network audio infrastructure. For churches adding comprehensive digital audio networking, the SQ-6 provides more robust expansion possibilities.

The SQ-5 handles typical digital stage box deployments fine—one or two stage boxes with standard audio networking. If you anticipate growing to five or more networked devices or supporting multiple separate worship spaces simultaneously, the SQ-6 provides a more scalable platform.

## Backup and Redundancy

If your church needs backup mixing capability, consider what happens when your primary mixer fails. An SQ-6 backup can cover for an SQ-5 in emergency—their architectures are fundamentally similar, and your shows transfer between them. However, recalling an SQ-6 show on an SQ-5 console requires some adjustment, as the channel counts don't match.

Ideally, backup consoles match primary consoles, or you maintain two identical consoles simultaneously. For larger installations where mixing failure means service cancellation or serious problems, maintaining a second SQ-6 provides true redundancy.

## Cost Considerations

The SQ-6 commands a substantial price premium over the SQ-5. This isn't purely about size—you're paying for expanded processing power, additional I/O, and more sophisticated networking capabilities. For churches with limited budgets, the SQ-5 provides exceptional value. For churches that can afford the SQ-6's premium and genuinely need its expanded capacity, the investment yields years of reliable service.

Some churches start with an SQ-5 and upgrade to an SQ-6 after growth necessitates it. This staged approach spreads costs over time and allows your team to grow into digital mixing gradually. However, reselling used SQ-5 consoles can be challenging, so plan carefully if you anticipate upgrading.

## Training and Team Knowledge

Both consoles share fundamentally similar interfaces and workflows. A team trained on the SQ-5 transitions to the SQ-6 easily. However, the SQ-6's expanded options mean you have more advanced features available if your team develops expertise.

For churches with less experienced mixing teams, the SQ-5's smaller channel count actually helps learning. Fewer options mean fewer ways to accidentally create problems. As your team builds expertise, the SQ-6's additional flexibility becomes more valuable.

## Right-Sizing Your Console Choice

Choose the SQ-5 if: Your current worship setup uses fewer than 20 channels regularly, you have limited control room space, you value portability, you want the most cost-effective Allen & Heath digital console, or your mixing team is relatively new to digital mixing.

Choose the SQ-6 if: You run large complex worship bands regularly exceeding 24 channels, you need sophisticated simultaneous monitor mixes, you require redundant processing capacity for reliability, you have dedicated control room space, or you anticipate substantial growth in the next three to five years.

Your chosen console will serve your ministry faithfully for many years. Thoughtful selection now ensures you have the right tool for your current reality and reasonable growth. Both are excellent choices that deliver professional-grade audio quality to your congregation.
