# Behringer X32 Setup Guide for Churches

The Behringer X32 is one of the most popular digital mixers in churches across North America. Its blend of affordability, reliability, and comprehensive feature set makes it ideal for mid-sized congregations and streaming setups. If you've just acquired an X32 or inherited one from a previous tech director, this guide will walk you through the essential setup steps to get sound flowing to your congregation and potentially your online audience.

## Understanding the Physical Layout

The X32 is a 32-channel, 6-bus digital mixing console with an imposing control surface that might seem intimidating at first glance. The mixer's layout follows a logical left-to-right flow. On the left side, you'll find 32 channel faders arranged in 8 groups of 4. Above each fader sits a rotary encoder that handles channel selection and parameter adjustment. The center section contains your main bus faders, transport controls, and navigation buttons. The right side houses the master output section with monitoring options and your main output faders for the left and right main speakers.

The X32's rear panel is where all the real connections happen. You have 32 XLR input channels arranged in blocks (channels 1-8, 9-16, 17-24, 25-32). Most churches use the first 8 channels for their primary inputs—worship leader's vocal microphone, drums, guitars, keyboards, and any additional instruments. The remaining channels can handle overflow or be left unused. You'll also find six 1/4-inch TRS outputs for your main stereo pair (L/R), subwoofer, and auxiliary outputs for monitors or recording.

## Powering On and Initial Setup

Before connecting any audio devices, use the included power cable to connect the X32 to a standard wall outlet. Locate the power switch on the rear panel and turn it on. The screen will boot up and display the main mixing interface. This usually takes about 10-15 seconds. Once booted, the screen shows you the current active input channel at the top, your metering information on the left, and the main mix view on the right.

After powering on for the first time, you'll want to set the system sample rate. Go to the Setup menu by pressing the Setup button on the console. Navigate to Audio to confirm your sample rate is set to 48 kHz, which is the standard for most church applications. This is the universal sample rate for AV production and ensures compatibility with video, streaming software, and recording equipment.

## Channel Strip Basics

Each of the 32 input channels on the X32 follows the same signal flow, which is the foundation of understanding how to route audio. Starting from the top of the channel strip on the screen, you'll see the input channel number and its assigned name. You can rename channels to match your setup—for example, "Lead Vocal," "Kick Drum," "Keys"—which makes it much easier for volunteers to navigate.

Below the channel name is the gain knob (often called the preamp gain). This is where you set the input level for each microphone or instrument. The goal is to set this so that loud peaks on your source (a sung note, a drum hit) reach approximately -12 dB to -6 dB on the channel meter. This is crucial for maintaining clean audio without distortion. Too low, and you'll need to push everything in the mix later, making the signal noisy. Too high, and you'll get distortion that no amount of fader adjustment can fix.

Below the gain section, you'll find the channel fader, which controls how loud that channel is in the main mix. The fader's 0 dB position (marked with a line) is considered unity gain—the point where the channel contributes its natural level to the mix. Channels used for critical audio sources should typically sit around this 0 dB mark during a service.

The small buttons below or near each channel control what happens to that channel's signal. The most important button is the one that routes the channel to your main output (usually labeled "Main" or "Output"). By default, when you set up the console, all input channels should be routed to the main output.

## Routing Microphones and DI Boxes

Most churches have a standard setup for microphone channels. Your lead vocalist's microphone goes to channel 1, which you'll want to rename "Lead Vocal" for clarity. A handheld microphone for announcements or testimonies might go to channel 2. Instrument microphones (drums, guitar amp, bass amp) fill out channels 3 through 8. If you have a keyboard, most keyboards have their own stereo output that you can plug into channels 15 and 16 (a stereo pair).

Digital Interface (DI) boxes are used to convert high-impedance signals (like keyboards or acoustic guitars with pickups) into balanced low-impedance signals that the X32's microphone inputs expect. Simply connect the DI box's XLR output to an available input channel on the X32, then plug the instrument into the DI box's 1/4-inch input jack.

To route a channel to your main output, select that channel by pressing one of the physical faders on the mixer. The screen will highlight that channel's information. Look for a button labeled "Out" or "Main" (the exact label depends on your firmware version). When you press it, you should see the channel's fader level displayed on the main mix view, and you should see a green indicator showing it's routed to the main output. This is the fundamental routing operation that gets audio from the microphone to your speakers.

## Getting Sound to Your Main Speakers

Your X32's main stereo output should be connected to an external power amplifier or powered speakers. These connections are on the rear panel, typically labeled "OUT L" and "OUT R." Use balanced XLR cables to connect from the X32 to your amplifier, then connect the amplifier to your main speakers.

Once the physical connections are made, you need to ensure the main output level is set correctly. On the mixer's right side, you'll see the main L/R faders. These control the overall volume going to your congregation's speakers. Set these faders to approximately 0 dB (unity gain) initially. The actual volume heard in the room is controlled by the gain on your power amplifier, which you should set to about three-quarters of its maximum for typical services.

When you have microphones plugged in and channels routed to the main output, you should see signal moving up the main output meters when someone speaks into a mic. If you see no signal, double-check that the channel's gain is set appropriately (not too low), that the channel fader is not at the bottom, and that the channel is routed to the main output.

## Testing Your Setup

Before your first service, test the entire signal chain with your team. Have your lead vocalist speak into the vocal microphone while you watch the channel 1 fader. Adjust the gain until the vocalist's peak levels sit comfortably in the yellow zone on the meter (around -12 dB). Then adjust the channel fader to a level that sounds good in the room. Repeat this process for every input channel you plan to use.

If you're planning to stream your service, you'll need to route audio to your streaming software separately from your main output. This is handled through auxiliary buses, which is covered in our streaming guides. For now, focus on getting clean audio to your main speakers.

The X32 is a powerful tool, and this guide covers the essentials to get you mixing. As you grow more comfortable, you'll explore scenes to save your Sunday setups, effects to enhance your audio, and remote control options to manage the mix from front-of-house or backstage. Welcome to the X32 family—you've made a solid choice for your church's audio needs.
