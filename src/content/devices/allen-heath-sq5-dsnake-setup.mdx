# Allen & Heath dSNAKE Digital Snake Setup for Churches

The Allen & Heath dSNAKE system represents a fundamental shift in how churches handle stage audio connectivity. Rather than running dozens of individual analog microphone cables from your stage to the mixing console location—a cable management nightmare that becomes increasingly impractical in larger sanctuaries or multi-room facilities—you run a single Ethernet cable carrying all your stage audio signals. This elegant approach drastically simplifies your physical infrastructure while providing superior audio quality and unprecedented flexibility.

## Understanding dSNAKE Technology

Digital audio networking has transformed professional live sound. The dSNAKE system, proprietary to Allen & Heath, packages multiple audio signals into a single Ethernet connection using a specialized protocol optimized for ultra-low latency and rock-solid reliability. Unlike general-purpose networking protocols, dSNAKE is engineered specifically for live event audio, meaning your mixing experience is indistinguishable from having all your stage microphones plugged directly into the console.

The dSNAKE cable itself looks identical to a standard Ethernet patch cable, but it carries your complete stage audio infrastructure. A dSNAKE stage box might have 16 analog audio inputs and 8 outputs, all accessible to the SQ-5 as if they were local inputs, despite being physically located on your stage a hundred feet away.

## dSNAKE vs GigACE: Choosing Your Approach

Allen & Heath offers two digital networking options for the SQ-5: dSNAKE and GigACE. Understanding their differences helps you choose the right approach for your church's specific needs and growth trajectory.

dSNAKE is Allen & Heath's proprietary format designed for legacy compatibility and simplicity. If you already own Allen & Heath equipment with dSNAKE capability, or if you want a straightforward system without deep networking knowledge, dSNAKE works beautifully. A single dSNAKE cable carries audio in both directions—inputs from the stage box to the mixer and outputs from the mixer back to stage monitors.

GigACE, by contrast, uses standard Gigabit Ethernet networking protocols, making it more flexible for complex multi-location setups and easier to integrate with other manufacturer's equipment. If your church anticipates future expansion involving equipment from different manufacturers or running multiple networks simultaneously, GigACE provides more architectural flexibility. However, GigACE requires more networking expertise to configure properly.

For most churches starting their digital networking journey, dSNAKE offers the simplest, most reliable path forward.

## Planning Your dSNAKE Deployment

Before running cables, determine how many stage inputs you actually need. A typical small church might use a dSNAKE box with eight inputs: lead vocal microphone, backup vocal microphone, acoustic guitar, keyboard, drums (either a single overhead or kick drum mic), acoustic guitar, and perhaps two ambient microphones. A larger church might need 16 inputs or even multiple dSNAKE boxes for complex band setups.

Count your current and foreseeable future inputs generously. It's far easier to install extra dSNAKE capacity than to run additional cables later. Plan your cable routing from the stage location to your mixer location—ideally, under floorboards or along cable trays rather than across walkways where people might trip.

## Installing dSNAKE Stage Boxes

A dSNAKE stage box is a rugged, typically rack-mounted or floor-standing unit. Position it on stage in a central location relative to your microphones. Each input on the stage box has both XLR connections and connector labels. Begin connecting your microphones to the stage box inputs, using the same care you'd use with any microphone connection. Ensure connections are tight and properly seated.

The stage box requires power—either through a standard wall outlet or sometimes from the dSNAKE cable itself in certain configurations. Check your specific stage box model for power requirements. Connect power and verify the stage box powers on. You should see indicator lights showing that the box is ready for network connection.

## Running the dSNAKE Cable

Now comes the physical infrastructure: running the dSNAKE cable from the stage box to your mixer location. The dSNAKE cable itself is remarkably robust and can span up to 300 feet, depending on cable quality and specific model. Run it through cable trays, conduit, or other protective routing to prevent physical damage.

Avoid running dSNAKE cables next to high-power AC lines or RF equipment, as interference can degrade audio quality. If you must cross high-power cables, do so at right angles and maintain distance whenever possible. In most church environments, this consideration is minor, but in facilities with extensive amplifier racks and RF systems, it becomes important.

At the mixer end, connect the dSNAKE cable to one of the dSNAKE ports on your SQ-5. The mixer has dedicated dSNAKE connectors specifically for this purpose. Ensure the connection is fully seated and secured.

## SQ-5 dSNAKE Configuration

With the physical cable in place, configure the SQ-5 to recognize and work with your dSNAKE stage box. Navigate to the System Settings on the touchscreen and look for the dSNAKE section. The mixer should automatically detect the connected stage box and list its inputs in your channel pool.

Once the stage box is recognized, its inputs appear in the channel selection interface exactly as if they were local channels. You can assign them to mix buses, apply processing, and control them just like any other audio input. The transparency of this integration is remarkable—after initial setup, you largely forget that your stage inputs are connected via network rather than analog cables.

## Creating a Logical Channel Organization

With dSNAKE enabled, establish a clear naming convention for your stage box inputs in the SQ-5. Rather than generic labels like "Stage Input 1," use descriptive names: "Vocals - Lead," "Drums - Kick," "Keys - Main." This organization pays dividends when volunteers operate the mixer or when you add additional team members.

Consider grouping related inputs in your channel layout. Keep all vocal inputs adjacent on your console, all instrument inputs in their own section. This logical organization matches how musicians think about their instruments and makes the mixing flow more intuitive.

## Troubleshooting dSNAKE Signal Path

Occasionally, stage inputs might not appear in your mixer's channel list. Start with physical verification: walk to the stage box and confirm all connections are tight. Check that the dSNAKE cable hasn't been accidentally disconnected at either end. Visually inspect the cable for any signs of damage.

If physical connections are solid but the mixer still doesn't recognize the stage box, power cycle both devices. Turn off the stage box completely, wait ten seconds, then power it back on. Wait for its indicator lights to stabilize. Then power cycle the SQ-5. This resets the network negotiation and often resolves recognition issues.

If problems persist, navigate to the System Settings on the mixer and check that dSNAKE is explicitly enabled. Some configurations default to disabled status for devices not actively in use. Enable dSNAKE and save the configuration.

## Adding Multiple dSNAKE Boxes

As your church grows, you might add a second dSNAKE stage box for even more input capacity. The SQ-5 supports multiple dSNAKE connections simultaneously. Configure the second box through the same dSNAKE settings menu, and its inputs will appear alongside your first stage box's inputs.

This expandability means you can start with a single stage box and scale up incrementally as your mixing needs grow, without replacing your console or redesigning your infrastructure.

## Monitoring and Maintenance

dSNAKE systems are remarkably reliable, but like any network infrastructure, they benefit from periodic attention. Check that all connections remain tight during setup. After your first service using dSNAKE, listen carefully to stage inputs for any noise, dropouts, or quality issues. Most problems emerge immediately and indicate cable or connection issues rather than fundamental system problems.

Keep spare dSNAKE cables on hand in your equipment closet. They're inexpensive insurance against service disruptions if a cable becomes damaged. Include a brief troubleshooting note with your spare cables describing common fixes your team should try first.
