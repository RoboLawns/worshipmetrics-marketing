# XR12 vs XR18: Which Behringer X-Air for Your Church?

Both the Behringer XR12 and XR18 are headless, tablet-controlled digital mixers in the X-Air family. They share the same fundamental architecture—no physical control surface, complete control through the X Air app, networked operation. The differences are in channel count, price, and intended venue size. For many churches, the choice comes down to how many audio inputs you need and what your budget allows.

## Channel Count Comparison

The XR12 provides 12 input channels. The XR18 provides 16 input channels. This four-channel difference is the primary distinguishing factor between the two models.

For a very small church with 6-10 active inputs (perhaps 3 microphones, 2 guitars, 2 keyboards, 1 click track, plus ambient room mic), the XR12's 12 channels are more than sufficient. You have room for growth and backup channels.

For a small-to-mid-sized church with 12-14 active inputs (perhaps 4 microphones, 3 guitars, 2 keyboards, 1 drums input, 1-2 backing tracks, 1 ambient mic), the XR12 still works, but you're at capacity. Any additional input requires careful channel management or unused inputs.

For a church already using 15 channels or anticipating growth to that point, the XR18's 16 channels provide comfortable headroom.

## Price Difference

The XR12 costs significantly less than the XR18, typically $200-300 cheaper. For churches with limited budgets, this difference is meaningful. If you're not certain you need 16 channels, the XR12's lower price is attractive.

The price-to-channel ratio is better on the XR18, but that's only valuable if you need those channels. Paying for 16 channels when you only use 12 is wasteful.

## Physical Size and Portability

Both the XR12 and XR18 are rackmount units occupying 1U of space. They're virtually identical in physical dimensions—roughly the size of a small router. This isn't a meaningful differentiator between them.

Both are portable enough to move between rooms or campuses. Neither is difficult to transport.

## Mixing Capability and Features

The XR12 and XR18 share the same mixing engine. The effects, EQ, compression, routing, and all mixing features are identical. From a pure capability perspective, they're equivalent. The only difference is the number of simultaneous inputs you can handle.

Both support USB audio, multi-track recording, scene management, and all the professional features you'd expect from Behringer equipment.

## App Control and Operation

The X Air app works identically on both. Whether you're mixing the XR12 or XR18, the app experience is the same. The interface adapts to show 12 or 16 channels depending on which mixer is connected, but operation is consistent.

This also means your knowledge transfers between models. If you learn on the XR12 and later upgrade to the XR18, you're immediately comfortable with the larger mixer.

## Streaming and Recording

Both models support USB audio for streaming and recording equally well. You can set up independent streaming auxiliary mixes on both. From a streaming perspective, they're equivalent.

The XR12 might be slightly more attractive for streaming-only applications where you don't need many channels but do need the streaming capability. The XR18 is better if you're mixing both live and streaming with many active channels.

## Growth Considerations

The XR12 is ideal if you're just getting started, have a small congregation, or have a fixed venue size where you know you'll never need more than 12 channels. Many churches buy the XR12 intending to stay with it permanently.

The XR18 is better if you anticipate growth. If you're adding worship services, launching a satellite campus, or expanding your band, the extra four channels provide headroom.

If you start with the XR12 and grow beyond it, upgrading to the XR18 is straightforward. Your scenes and settings transfer directly. You lose nothing by starting small.

## Deciding Which Is Right for You

Choose the XR12 if:

Your church has 10 or fewer active audio inputs. Your budget is limited and price matters. You want the simplest, most affordable digital mixing solution. You're confident you won't grow beyond 12 channels in the foreseeable future.

Choose the XR18 if:

Your church currently uses or plans to use 13+ audio inputs. You want headroom for future growth. You're mixing a large worship band with many separate instruments. You already have a physical console with 16+ channels in use and want to replicate that capability.

## The Honest Truth

For most small churches, the XR12 is the right choice. Twelve channels cover nearly all small worship setups. You save $200-300 compared to the XR18. The mixer is slightly more compact and feels less overwhelming with fewer channels to manage.

The XR18 is the right choice only if you have a genuine need for more than 12 channels. Don't buy extra channels you don't use. Buy only what you need.

## Making Your Choice

Before deciding, count your active audio inputs during a typical Sunday. Lead vocal, guitars, drums (usually 1-2 inputs), keyboards (usually 1-2 inputs), backing tracks, ambient mic, click track. Add this all up. If the total is 12 or under, the XR12 is perfect. If it's 13-16, go with the XR18.

Talk to other churches using each mixer. Their real-world experiences inform your decision better than specs do.

## The Scalability Question

X-Air mixers are built for portability and small venues. If your church is already large enough to need more than 16 channels, consider whether an X32 Compact or full X32 might better suit your long-term needs. The X32 family offers more faders, more physical control, and room to grow beyond what even the XR18 provides.

For small churches, though, the XR12 and XR18 are perfect. They deliver professional mixing capability at an affordable price. The choice between them is simple: do you need 16 channels or 12? If 12 is enough, the XR12 is the smarter buy. If you need 16, the XR18 is worth the extra cost.

Welcome to the X-Air family, whichever model you choose. You've made an excellent decision for your church's audio infrastructure.
