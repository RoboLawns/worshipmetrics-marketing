Your stream audio has a loud echo or feedback loop — sometimes the whole congregation hears itself a split-second delayed, or worse, it builds into a loud squeal. This usually happens when the room's audio is being picked up by a microphone and sent back out through speakers, creating a closed loop. We can break that loop in seconds.

## The Most Common Cause: Room Microphone Picking Up Stream Audio

The most likely culprit is a **room microphone or broadcast speaker picking up the live stream audio**, which then gets sent back into the stream:

1. **Identify the room mic**: Is there a wireless lavalier on the pastor, a ceiling mic, or a room ambient mic feeding into your mixer?
2. **Locate the broadcast speaker**: Where are you monitoring the stream (in the booth, on a speaker in the room, etc.)?
3. **Separate them physically**: Move the room microphone away from any speakers playing the stream back
4. **Mute the stream's audio during service**: In OBS or your mixer, ensure the live stream audio *isn't* feeding into the room's speakers (only use it for online viewers, not for the in-person congregation)

If the pastor is wearing a wireless mic and you're playing back the stream audio on a speaker in the sanctuary, the room mic will pick it up and cause feedback. **Solution:** Monitor the stream on headphones in the booth only, not on a room speaker.

## Check OBS Monitoring Settings

If you're using OBS and monitoring your mixed audio while streaming:

1. In OBS, click **Settings > Audio**
2. Look for **Monitoring Device** settings
3. If monitoring is enabled, verify it's set to **Headphones** or a **specific output device** — NOT to the system speaker
4. If monitoring is routed to the room speaker, that audio feeds back to your mics and causes echo
5. Disable monitoring entirely if you don't need to hear the mix while streaming

**Better practice:** Use **headphone monitoring** only, and never route stream audio to a room speaker during the service.

## Disable PC Audio Feedback in OBS

OBS sometimes creates a feedback loop if the mic is plugged into the PC and OBS is also monitoring PC audio:

1. In OBS, go to **Settings > Audio**
2. Under **Audio Monitoring**, set it to **Monitor Off** (or set it to headphones, not speakers)
3. Verify **Desktop Audio** source in OBS is **muted** if you don't need system audio on the stream
4. Test the stream — echo should stop

## Use a Feedback Suppressor in Your Mixer

If the echo persists despite separating mics and speakers, use your mixer's built-in feedback suppressor:

1. In your mixer (Behringer X32, Yamaha TF1, etc.), access the channel that's feeding the stream
2. Look for **Feedback Suppressor**, **Anti-Feedback**, or **Notch Filter**
3. Enable it and adjust the frequency to target the feedback tone (usually a high-pitched squeal in the 2–4kHz range)
4. Test and fine-tune
5. As a fallback, enable a **high-pass filter** on room mics to cut low frequencies (feedback is usually high-frequency)

This is a last-resort fix — better to eliminate the feedback source itself.

## Check for Multiple Audio Path Loops

If you have multiple streams or outputs going to different places, verify none of them create a loop:

- **Is the stream audio feeding back into the mixer?** Check your OBS audio sources — make sure you're not sending the stream output *back into* the mixer input.
- **Are there multiple recording or streaming outputs?** Make sure only one is active at a time.
- **Is the ATEM or other hardware device creating a loop?** Verify audio routing in the switcher — don't route the stream output back to the input.

## Test Before the Service

Before Sunday:

1. Do a 2–3 minute test stream with everyone in the sanctuary
2. Have someone outside the building watch the stream on a phone or computer
3. Ask them to confirm: "Do you hear any echo, delay, or feedback?"
4. If yes, perform the fixes above
5. Test again
6. Once clean, you're safe to go live

## Wireless Mic Specific: Check Channel Separation

If you're using wireless microphones, ensure they're not interfering:

1. Verify wireless mic frequencies don't overlap with streaming audio frequencies
2. If you have multiple wireless mics, ensure they're on *different* frequencies
3. Check if a wireless mic is accidentally picking up the stream's audio output (this happens if a monitor speaker is too close to a mic receiver)
4. Move wireless mic receivers away from any room speakers
