Your HDMI feed cuts out intermittently or shows a black screen, then comes back. It's usually happening during a key moment — choir entrance, pastor speaking, or important visual. HDMI dropouts are almost always cable-related, but HDCP handshake and EDID confusion can also cause blackouts. Let's get your signal stable.

## Replace the HDMI Cable Immediately

The most common cause of intermittent HDMI dropout is a degraded or defective cable:

1. Unplug the current HDMI cable from both ends
2. Use a **high-quality HDMI 2.0 cable** — not a cheap dollar-store cable or a 10-year-old one gathering dust
3. Firmly push the connectors into both the source device (camera) and the receiving device (switcher/capture card) until you feel them click and lock
4. Test the video — the signal should stabilize immediately
5. If you don't have a spare cable, borrow one from another part of your setup to test

**HDMI connector tip:** Make sure the cable isn't being bent at a sharp angle at either end. Keep it relatively straight, especially near the connectors.

## Check Cable Length

HDMI has distance limitations. If your cable is very long, signal degradation happens:

- **Standard HDMI cable**: reliable up to about 25 meters
- **Longer distances**: requires an **HDMI repeater** or **HDMI booster** (£20–50)
- **Professional solution**: use **SDI** (Serial Digital Interface) instead, which handles longer runs without active equipment

If your cable is running more than 15 meters, invest in an HDMI repeater or switch to SDI for reliability.

## Verify Connections Are Fully Seated

A slightly loose HDMI plug is a frequent culprit:

1. Power off the source device (camera)
2. Completely unplug the HDMI cable
3. Look at both connectors: they should be clean and undamaged
4. Inspect the ports on both the camera and switcher for bent pins or debris
5. Firmly plug the HDMI cable back in — you should feel a definite click and see the connector lock into place
6. Power the camera back on
7. Test the video

If the connector feels loose or wiggles, the cable or port may be damaged. Test with a new cable.

## Disable HDCP If Possible (And Permitted)

HDCP (High-bandwidth Digital Content Protection) is a copy-protection handshake that sometimes fails and causes black screens. This is most common when connecting certain cameras to switchers:

1. If your *camera* has an HDCP setting, try disabling it:
   - Access the camera's web interface or menu
   - Look for **HDCP** or **Copy Protection**
   - Set it to **Disabled** or **Off**
2. If your *switcher* has an HDCP option, disable it too (check the manual)
3. Restart both devices and test

**Important:** Only disable HDCP if you're not capturing copyrighted content for distribution. In churches, you're typically fine, but check if you're streaming copyrighted worship material.

## Resolve EDID (Extended Display Identification Data) Issues

EDID is how devices communicate their capabilities (resolution, refresh rate, etc.). Mismatched EDID can cause the handshake to fail and drop the signal:

1. Power off the switcher or capture device (the destination)
2. Unplug the HDMI cable completely
3. Power on the destination device *first*
4. Then plug in the HDMI cable
5. Then power on the source (camera)

This forces the source to read EDID from the destination in the correct order. Often this alone fixes intermittent blackouts.

**Alternative:** If that doesn't work, access the camera's video output settings and manually set the resolution and refresh rate to match what the switcher expects (usually 1080p 60Hz or 1080p 50Hz).

## Use an HDMI Repeater or Booster

If your cable is long or if the signal is marginal:

1. Purchase an **HDMI repeater** or **HDMI booster** (not an HDMI splitter — different device)
2. Place it roughly halfway along the cable run
3. Power it via USB (if it requires power)
4. This regenerates the HDMI signal and can solve dropout issues on marginal installations

Repeaters typically cost £20–50 and are worth the investment if you're running cable through walls or across a large sanctuary.

## Test with a Known-Good Spare Cable

Before the service:

1. Borrow an HDMI cable you know works from another part of your setup (e.g., from a projector or media player)
2. Unplug the questionable cable and plug in the spare
3. Run the service or do a test stream
4. If the spare cable works flawlessly for 30 minutes, the original cable is the problem — replace it permanently
5. If the spare cable also drops out, the issue is EDID, HDCP, or port damage (not the cable)

## Power Cycle in the Right Order

Sometimes HDMI handshakes get stuck. Perform a clean power cycle:

1. Turn off the camera first
2. Turn off the switcher/capture device second
3. Wait 30 seconds
4. Power on the switcher first
5. Power on the camera second
6. Wait 10 seconds for both devices to fully boot
7. Test the signal

This ensures the destination device is ready before the source tries to handshake with it.

---

**If signal is stable during testing but drops during the live service**, the issue may be heat. Make sure both devices have good ventilation and aren't overheating. A camera that's been on for 3+ hours may overheat and drop HDMI signal — temporarily power it off for 30 seconds to cool, then resume.
