Your IT person says your internet is "fast," but you're not sure if it's fast *enough* for streaming. You're worried about buffering, dropped frames, or the stream going offline mid-service. Let's calculate your actual bandwidth needs and test your connection properly.

## The Simple Answer: You Need Upload Speed, Not Download Speed

Most speed tests show download speed (fast). Streaming needs **upload speed** (slow on most connections):

- **Download speed:** How fast you download from the internet (usually 50–500 Mbps for home/small business)
- **Upload speed:** How fast you send to the internet (usually 5–20 Mbps)
- **Streaming uses:** Upload speed only

This is the key insight that surprises most people.

## Calculate Your Streaming Bitrate Requirements

Different stream quality needs different bitrate (amount of data sent per second):

| Quality | Bitrate | Min Upload Speed |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| 480p 30fps | 1500 kbps | 2.2 Mbps |
| 720p 30fps | 2500 kbps | 3.75 Mbps |
| 720p 60fps | 4500 kbps | 6.75 Mbps |
| 1080p 30fps | 5000 kbps | 7.5 Mbps |
| 1080p 60fps | 8000 kbps | 12 Mbps |

**Rule of thumb:** Your actual upload speed should be **1.5x your streaming bitrate**. So:
- To stream 720p safely, you need upload speed of at least **3.75 Mbps** (1.5 × 2500 kbps)
- To stream 1080p safely, you need upload speed of at least **7.5 Mbps** (1.5 × 5000 kbps)

The 1.5x headroom accounts for network variation and overhead.

## Test Your Upload Speed Properly

Don't just run a random speed test. Test *during* your service time, from your streaming PC:

**Wrong way:**
- Run speedtest.net on your phone while someone else is browsing the web
- Result: 50 Mbps down, 8 Mbps up — looks good!
- Problem: You didn't measure what your streaming PC will actually see

**Right way:**
1. On your streaming PC, close all other programs and browser tabs
2. Have staff close any downloads, backups, or large file operations
3. Go to **speedtest.net** in a browser
4. Run the test
5. Note the **upload** speed (e.g., 6.5 Mbps)
6. Run it 2–3 times and take the average (network varies)

Now you have a realistic measure of what your streaming PC can use.

## What Can Safely Use the Remaining Bandwidth?

If your upload is 10 Mbps and you need 5 Mbps for streaming (1080p):

**Remaining: 5 Mbps for everything else.**

What competes for this 5 Mbps during the service:
- Congregation Wi-Fi (staff browsing, phones, etc.)
- Security cameras uploading to the cloud
- Automatic backups (Windows updates, file syncs)
- Anyone else on the network

**Strategy: Reserve bandwidth for streaming.**

If streaming is critical, ensure it gets priority:
1. **Disable automatic updates** during service (Windows, antivirus)
2. **Pause cloud backups** during service
3. **Limit congregation Wi-Fi** or tell staff not to download during the service
4. **Separate networks:** If possible, put streaming on its own internet connection or wired connection isolated from Wi-Fi

## For Multiple Simultaneous Streams

If you're streaming to YouTube *and* Facebook at the same time:

- YouTube 1080p: 5000 kbps
- Facebook 1080p: 5000 kbps
- **Total: 10,000 kbps = 10 Mbps**
- **Minimum upload needed: 15 Mbps** (1.5 × 10 Mbps)

This is why many churches stream to one platform at a time, or use **Restream** (which only requires one upload stream that it redistributes).

## What If Your Upload Is Too Low?

If you only have 3 Mbps upload but need 7.5 Mbps for 1080p:

**Option 1: Lower the quality**
- Stream 720p instead of 1080p (2500 kbps = 3.75 Mbps needed)
- Viewers can't tell the difference on a phone anyway

**Option 2: Lower the frame rate**
- Stream 24fps instead of 30fps
- Slightly lower bandwidth, barely noticeable

**Option 3: Upgrade your internet**
- Contact your ISP and request a higher upload speed tier
- Business-class internet usually has better upload speeds
- May cost more but worth it if streaming is important

**Option 4: Use Restream**
- Stream once to Restream (one bitrate requirement)
- Restream handles sending to multiple platforms
- More reliable overall

## Monitor Bandwidth During Service

Keep an eye on upload speed during the live stream:

1. On a separate device (phone, tablet), run a speed test periodically during the service
2. If upload drops below your required bitrate, your stream will suffer
3. This helps you identify congestion times (e.g., "Every Sunday at 10:30am, someone starts a large download")

Identifying patterns helps you troubleshoot and plan.

## Bandwidth Load During Service

Realistic bandwidth usage during a 1-hour service:

| Activity | Upload Used |
|----------|------------|
| Streaming 1080p | 5 Mbps |
| Congregation Wi-Fi (light browsing) | 0.5 Mbps |
| Cloud backup/sync | 1 Mbps |
| Security cameras (if cloud-based) | 0.5 Mbps |
| **Total** | **7 Mbps** |

If your total is 7 Mbps and you only have 8 Mbps upload, you're at 87% capacity — any spike causes problems.

**Better scenario:** You have 12 Mbps upload for the same usage (60% capacity).

## Business Internet vs Home Internet

**Home internet (typical):**
- Download: 100–300 Mbps
- Upload: 5–20 Mbps
- Stability: Good for most tasks, but can be throttled during peak hours

**Business internet (better for streaming):**
- Download: 50–300 Mbps
- Upload: 5–50 Mbps
- Stability: Consistent; less likely to be throttled
- Priority: Higher priority than residential customers

If streaming is central to your ministry, business internet is worth the extra cost (usually £30–60/month more).

## Quick Bandwidth Checklist

Before your first stream:

1. [ ] Measured upload speed during peak hours (when everyone is using the network)
2. [ ] Calculated required bitrate for desired quality (use table above)
3. [ ] Confirmed upload is 1.5x the bitrate needed
4. [ ] Identified what other services compete for bandwidth
5. [ ] Planned to pause non-essential services (updates, backups) during service
6. [ ] Did a 30+ minute test stream to confirm stability
7. [ ] Identified backup plan if bandwidth degrades (lower quality, pause Wi-Fi, etc.)
