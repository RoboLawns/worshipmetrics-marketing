## Starting From Practically Nothing

A church plant has zero infrastructure, zero equipment, and approximately zero budget. But it often has one thing a growing church doesn't: urgency. People are gathering, stories are being told, community is being built, and someone realizes that members who moved away or can't attend in person want to be part of it too. So you need to stream, and you need to start this Sunday.

This is the honest conversation about streaming on a shoestring. You're not going to have multiple cameras. You're not going to have a fancy switcher or professional mixer. You might not have reliable internet. What you're going to do is show people that church is real, authentic, and worth being part of—even if the production quality is basic.

The equipment investment for a genuine streaming operation is under $500, and you can go even cheaper if you're willing to use what you already have. The actual limiting factor isn't gear—it's reliable internet and someone who's willing to operate the system every week.

## Choosing Your Camera (Or Not)

If someone on your plant team has a smartphone with a good camera, start there. A modern iPhone or Android phone mounted on a $20 tripod and positioned to see the front of your worship space is genuinely sufficient for version one. Yes, it's lower resolution than a dedicated camera. Yes, it's more prone to focus issues or audio problems. But it works, it's free, and it's immediately available.

If you're buying a dedicated camera, the Tenveo VHD30U is under $200 and is a massive step up from a smartphone. It's a compact PTZ camera that connects via HDMI, includes built-in audio, and can be positioned to capture your entire worship space. The microphone won't win awards, but it's adequate for a room with decent acoustics.

Your other option is to find a used consumer camcorder locally. Many churches have old DV camcorders in storage that they're happy to donate. These often output HDMI and are surprisingly capable for the price (or cost you nothing if you can get a donation).

Positioning matters more than the camera itself. Mount your camera high enough to see over people's heads. Frame the shot to include your speaker/worship leader and enough context that viewers understand what's happening. You're not trying to get a tight shot of the pastor's face. You're trying to show the authentic space where church is happening.

## Audio That Doesn't Sound Terrible

This is where cheap churches lose credibility fast. People will tolerate mediocre video. They won't tolerate audio that's inaudible, distorted, or inconsistent. This is where you spend your limited budget.

If your worship space has a basic PA system, connect your smartphone or camera directly to the PA output. Use a simple interface like the Behringer X-Air XR12 (under $300), which accepts both analog audio inputs and digital USB audio. Connect your wireless mic receiver to the XR12. Connect your laptop to the XR12 via USB. Now the audio in your stream is coming from your actual PA system, not from a camera microphone.

If you don't have a PA system yet, the same XR12 works. Connect your wireless receiver to it, send the audio to your laptop via USB, and output the signal to a pair of powered speakers for the room. One piece of gear solves both your in-room audio and your streaming audio.

The alternative (if your church plant is really bootstrapped) is a USB microphone like the Blue Yeti ($60-100). Mount it near your speaker, connect it to your streaming laptop, and you've got audio. It's not professional, but it's honest and it's clear.

## The Streaming Setup: OBS on a Borrowed Laptop

You need a laptop running OBS (free, open-source software). It doesn't need to be powerful. An older MacBook or Windows laptop that can run OBS is fine. Borrow one from a church member if you have to. You're spending a total of $0 on the software.

Open OBS. Create a single scene that captures your camera (via HDMI input or webcam input) and your audio (from the XR12 via USB). Add your church logo in the corner (design it for free on Canva if you don't have one already). Configure the output to stream to YouTube.

That's your setup. One camera, one microphone input, one OBS scene, one YouTube stream. No fancy transitions. No graphics. Just showing people what's happening in your church plant.

## YouTube as Your Streaming Platform

YouTube is free and powerful. Create a YouTube channel for your church. Enable live streaming (you may need to wait 24 hours after creating your account). Generate a stream key. Paste that key into OBS. Click "Start Streaming" and you're live.

YouTube is trustworthy. People know the platform. It has built-in chat and engagement tools. Your stream archives automatically, so people who can't watch live can watch the replay. And YouTube's algorithm sometimes promotes good content—if your plant has a compelling story or a great message, YouTube might actually help people discover you.

You can live stream directly from your phone too, if that's all you've got. Open YouTube on a smartphone, tap the camera icon, tap "Go Live," and you're streaming. It's not ideal (your phone's microphone will be your only audio source), but it works.

## Solving the Internet Problem

Your church plant is probably meeting in a borrowed space: a school cafeteria, a community center, or someone's backyard. These spaces rarely have reliable internet. This is your biggest technical risk.

Get a reliable mobile hotspot—like a Verizon or T-Mobile MiFi device. Commit to a data plan. If you're streaming a one-hour service weekly, you need about 2.5 GB of data per service (at standard video quality). Most unlimited cellular plans will cover this. The cost is $50-70 per month.

Test your cellular connection on the actual meeting space before Sunday. Set up your laptop and camera the day before and run a test stream. See if you get consistent bandwidth. If you get drops or significant buffering, find a better location or upgrade to a better cellular carrier.

Ideally, you find a physical space that already has fast, reliable internet. This might be a church that's sharing their facility, or a school with good bandwidth. Use that internet as your primary and keep cellular as your backup.

## The Sunday Morning Ritual

Thirty minutes before the service:

- Position your camera where it can see the front of the room.
- Connect your audio inputs to your mixer.
- Open OBS on your laptop.
- Connect to internet and verify bandwidth (Netflix speed test is fine).
- Start OBS preview mode to see what the camera sees.
- Check audio levels—speak into the microphone and watch the meters.
- Open YouTube and verify your stream key is correct.
- Post on your social media that you're going live in 10 minutes.
- Click "Start Streaming" in OBS.
- Send the YouTube link in a text message to everyone who wants to watch.

Five minutes before the service starts, everything is already live. People can join as the worship team comes up. When the service is over, click "Stop Streaming" in OBS and you're done.

## The Honest Limitations and How to Own Them

You're streaming one camera angle. The audio is decent but not pristine. The resolution might be 720p instead of 1080p. You don't have graphics, lower-thirds, or fancy transitions. Your stream probably stutters occasionally when someone's heavy phone usage impacts your internet.

Own this. Don't apologize for it, but don't hide from it either. Your church plant's authenticity is part of your appeal. You're not a polished, expensive production. You're a real community worshipping together. That's what people are actually looking for. Fancy production turns them away.

As your church grows, you'll upgrade. You'll add a second camera, a better audio system, a proper internet circuit. But right now, your goal is to gather people, build community, and show them the gospel is real. Everything else is secondary.

## Planning Your First Upgrade

After six months of streaming with a single camera and basic mixer, you'll know what matters for your specific situation. Maybe you'll discover that 80% of your online audience watches on mobile, which means you should optimize for vertical video. Maybe you'll realize your pastor walks around too much for a single static camera. Maybe you'll find that audio quality is what people complain about most, which means upgrading to a wireless mic system makes sense before buying another camera.

The advantage of starting small is that you learn what actually matters in your context before you spend thousands on gear that doesn't. You make investment decisions from experience rather than theory.

For now, show up, be authentic, stream reliably, and let the gospel do its work. The production quality will improve. The audience will grow. But none of that matters if you don't have the courage to start with what you have.
