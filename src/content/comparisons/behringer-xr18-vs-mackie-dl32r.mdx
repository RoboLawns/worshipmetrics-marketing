The Behringer XR18 and Mackie DL32R both prioritize networked audio control, but they serve different operational philosophies. The XR18 costs approximately $1200 and emphasizes remote mixing through tablets and phones. The DL32R costs around $2000 and emphasizes polished iPad integration through the Master Fader app. For churches exploring networked audio mixing, understanding which approach suits your team is important.

## Behringer X-Air XR18 Overview

The Behringer XR18 is a compact digital mixer designed for networked control. Rather than operating from the mixer itself, most control happens through the X-Air app on iPad, laptop, or phone. The physical mixer serves as a core processor and input/output hub, with actual mixing operated remotely from anywhere on your network.

This remote-first design is powerful. A sound engineer can operate from the sanctuary wearing a wireless headset, hearing the feed and adjusting monitors in real-time. They're not tied to a mixer location. For complex services with roaming speakers or multiple audio scenarios, this freedom is valuable.

The channel count is eighteen, which sounds limiting until you understand that you can have multiple mixing layers simultaneously. One operator might be managing the main mix while another controls stage monitors from their iPad. Multiple people can adjust the mix from different perspectives.

The ecosystem is the XR18's greatest strength. The community is enormous. Facebook groups dedicated to X-Air mixing are active and helpful. YouTube tutorials cover nearly every scenario. Other church sound engineers share configurations and tips constantly. If you're new to networked mixing, the XR18 community will help you get productive quickly.

The price is hard to beat. At $1200, you're getting professional digital mixing at an accessible cost. Combined with the strong community, the XR18 is the entry point for most churches exploring networked audio.

The trade-off is that operating entirely from a tablet requires getting comfortable mixing without physical faders. Some experienced audio engineers find this less intuitive than traditional mixing consoles. The iPad app is functional but not as polished as dedicated mixing interfaces.

## Mackie DL32R Overview

The Mackie DL32R is positioned as the premium networked mixer, emphasizing iPad control through the Master Fader app. Like the XR18, most operation happens remotely, but Mackie's approach is more refined.

The Master Fader app is the centerpiece. Mackie invested heavily in iOS integration, creating an app that feels like a native mixing console on iPad. The fader layout is intuitive, the visual design is polished, and the overall experience feels premium. If you're mixing from iPad, the DL32R's interface is noticeably better than competing apps.

The build quality is superior. The Mackie feels like professional equipment. The construction is solid, the components inspire confidence, and the physical design reflects careful engineering. This is equipment built for professional touring and installation use.

The channel count is thirty-two, providing more inputs than the XR18. The preamps are higher-quality than most competitors in this price range. For churches with larger audio demands, the additional channels and superior preamps matter.

The community is smaller than the XR18 but remains active. Mackie has invested in documentation and support, so you're not left struggling to figure things out. The professional touring community uses Mackie extensively, so professional-level resources are available.

The cost is higher — around $2000. For churches treating the mixer as a long-term investment, the superior build quality and refined iPad experience justify the premium.

The trade-off is that remote-only operation still requires comfort mixing without physical faders. The Master Fader app is excellent, but some audio engineers prefer traditional console control.

## Head-to-Head: Community Support vs Refined Experience

This comparison hinges on two factors: operational budget and mixing philosophy.

The XR18 wins decisively on price and community. If your church is budget-conscious and values peer support, the XR18 is clearly the choice. The larger community means faster problem-solving and more shared configurations. You'll feel less alone in learning networked mixing.

The DL32R wins on the mixing experience itself. The Master Fader app is noticeably more polished than the XR18's equivalent. If you're spending hours mixing from iPad, that interface quality accumulates into meaningful time savings and reduced frustration. The superior preamps are also genuinely better at handling the full range of audio sources churches use.

For churches where a volunteer sound engineer is learning networked mixing for the first time, the XR18's community advantage is material. They can find YouTube tutorials, ask questions in Facebook groups, and benefit from thousands of similar configurations.

For churches with experienced audio professionals who want the best possible mixing experience, the DL32R's refinement is worth the premium. The Master Fader app rewards expertise with elegant interface design.

## Which Should Your Church Choose?

**Choose Behringer XR18** if budget is the primary constraint. At $1200, the XR18 provides professional networked mixing at half the DL32R's cost. You're not getting inferior mixing capability; you're getting a simpler, more community-driven experience.

**Choose XR18** if your sound engineer is learning networked mixing for the first time. The abundance of YouTube tutorials, Facebook groups, and shared configurations makes learning faster and less frustrating. The community is genuinely supportive.

**Choose XR18** for smaller churches with simpler audio needs. The eighteen channels are adequate for most worship services, and the remote control is genuinely liberating.

**Choose Mackie DL32R** if audio mixing experience is your priority. If someone on your team is an experienced audio engineer and will be using the Master Fader app repeatedly, the refined interface is noticeably better. The time saved across hundreds of services adds up.

**Choose DL32R** if you want superior build quality and long-term reliability. The mixer is built for professional touring use, which means it's built to survive decades of heavy use.

**Choose DL32R** if you have more complex audio needs. The thirty-two channels and superior preamps handle larger events, multiple services running simultaneously, or complex audio scenarios better than the XR18.

**For most churches**, the XR18 is the practical choice. It delivers professional mixing, the community support is exceptional, and the savings can be invested in quality microphones or speakers that impact worship experience more directly.
