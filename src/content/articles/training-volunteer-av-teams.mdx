## Introduction

Volunteer AV teams are strongest when the system helps them succeed. Training is not only about teaching button presses. It is about building confidence, clarity, and repeatability.

Most congregation production problems are not caused by a lack of willing volunteers. They are caused by workflows that assume too much, document too little, and expect people to improvise under pressure.

## Define Roles Clearly

If one volunteer is expected to do audio, graphics, camera switching, and troubleshooting simultaneously, the problem is usually the workflow, not the volunteer.

Role clarity helps everyone. Before training begins, define:

- what each role is responsible for
- what each role is not responsible for
- what success looks like in that position
- who that person calls when something goes wrong

Clarity lowers anxiety and makes growth possible.

## Build a Runsheet for Real Humans

A runsheet should help someone keep up with the service, not overwhelm them with unnecessary detail. The best runsheets highlight timing, transitions, and role-specific responsibilities.

A useful runsheet should tell the team:

- what happens next
- who owns it
- what media or transition is involved
- what could go wrong at that moment
- what fallback plan exists if it does

If the runsheet only makes sense to the person who wrote it, it is not a real runsheet yet.

## Train in Layers

Volunteers do not need every advanced concept on day one. Start with the actions that matter most, then expand as confidence grows.

For most teams, that means:

- first learning the normal service flow
- then learning common recovery scenarios
- then learning how the system is connected
- then learning the why behind the workflow

Good training is progressive. It should not dump the entire AV stack on someone during their first rehearsal.

## Rehearse Handoffs and Failure Recovery

Training is incomplete if it never covers what happens when a slide deck stalls, a stream drops, or a mic stops working. Recovery is part of the real workflow.

That is why rehearsals should include:

- intentional role handoffs
- simulated small failures
- fallback communication patterns
- clear escalation paths

Teams become more confident when they know what to do after something unexpected happens, not just while everything is going smoothly.

## Keep Documentation Where Volunteers Can Use It

The best documentation is visible, current, and easy to reach under pressure. If it only exists in one person's notebook, it is not really documentation.

Useful volunteer documentation often includes:

- one-page quick start guides
- service-specific runsheets
- gear cheat sheets
- shutdown and restart instructions
- recovery notes for common issues

The goal is not to create documentation for its own sake. The goal is to reduce hesitation and dependency on memory.

## Build Confidence Through Repetition

Confidence does not come from one great training night. It comes from repeated success inside a stable workflow.

That means volunteer systems should prioritize:

- consistent terminology
- repeatable service patterns
- clean handoffs
- limited live surprises
- regular encouragement and review

People stay in volunteer roles longer when they feel prepared, supported, and successful.

## Conclusion

Healthy volunteer AV teams are built through repeatable workflows, role clarity, and systems that reduce stress instead of multiplying it.

The strongest training environments do not create super-operators. They create teams where ordinary volunteers can serve faithfully and confidently every week.
